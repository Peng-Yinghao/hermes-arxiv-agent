## Abstract

Abstract Model quantization has become essential for efficient large language model deployment, yet existing approaches present clear trade-offs: methods such as GPTQ and AWQ achieve practical compression but are lossy , while lossless techniques preserve fidelity but lack inference acceleration. This paper explores the middle ground of statistically-lossless compression , examining three complementary aspects of what losslessness means for quantized LLMs. First, task-lossless compression preserves zero-shot benchmark accuracy within natural sampling variance and is achievable at aggressive bitwidths. Second, we formalize the stricter notion of distribution-lossless compression, requiring the quantized model’s next-token distribution to be practically indistinguishable from the original, and propose the Expected Acceptance Rate (EAR) , the maximum token-agreement probability under optimal coupling, as a directly interpretable fidelity metric (e.g. EAR ≥ 0.99 \geq 0.99 means 99% agreement). Third, we prove a gamma-squared variance law showing that symmetric quantization inflates noise variance by γ 2 \gamma^{2} relative to asymmetric, making asymmetric quantization a prerequisite for distribution-lossless fidelity but not for task-level preservation. Through SLQ , a layer-wise non-uniform method with asymmetric quantization and wide bitwidth search, we obtain task-lossless compression at well below 4 bits per parameter (as low as 3.3 bits depending on the model), distribution-lossless compression at 5–6 bits per parameter on average, and inference speedups of 1.7 1.7 – 3.6 × 3.6\times compared to FP16 using optimized kernels. Source code is available at https://github.com/IST-DASLab/SLQ .

## 1 Introduction

The large computational and memory costs of large language model (LLM) inference have led model quantization, i.e. the reduction in bit-width for weights, activations, or both, to become a standard technique for efficient deployment.
As such, over the past few years, hundreds of LLM quantization techniques have been proposed, e.g. (Dettmers et al., 2022; Frantar et al., 2023; Chee et al., 2023; Tseng et al., 2024b; Kim et al., 2024; Malinovskii et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2024), exploring the trade-off curve between compression achieved and accuracy loss.

From the accuracy/compression perspective, existing approaches can be clustered into two categories.
The first is represented by *lossy compression techniques*, such as Round-to-Nearest (RTN) quantization (Dettmers et al., 2022), llama.cpp (Gerganov and llama.cpp contributors, 2023), GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023), or AWQ (Lin et al., 2024) which seek to map existing models to popular hardware-supported formats, such as 4-bit grouped weight quantization. These techniques have become extremely popular, and have widespread practical deployment, although they do decrease model accuracy: for example, a recent study by Kurtić et al. (2025) observed that methods such as GPTQ and AWQ can drop zero-shot accuracy on some complex tasks by 3-5% in the case of 4-bit weight quantization. More complex vector-quantized (Egiazarian et al., 2024; Tseng et al., 2024a) or trellis-based (Tseng et al., 2024b) methods can offer reduced accuracy loss at even lower bit-widths, but are hard to apply and interface with state-of-the-art inference pipelines such as vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023), where support for such complex techniques is currently lacking.

The second, more recent, category is given by methods which aim explicitly for lossless quantization of the original weights. For instance, ZipNN (Hershcovitch et al., 2025) observes that the exponents of LLM weights are highly compressible, as they do not use the entire bit-width range, reducing average size to approximately 11 bits per parameter (bpp) from 16 bpp, without accuracy loss. Other approaches include entropy-coded FP8 representations (Yang et al., 2025) and dynamic-length BF16 compression (Zhang et al., 2025). On the downside, lossless techniques struggle to reach high compression ratios and require complex kernel support for fast decoding at inference time.

Our Approach. In this paper, we focus on the gap between these two types of approaches, and investigate Statistically Lossless LLM Quantization (SLQ), which we define as compression that may deviate from perfectly matching outputs under deterministic decoding, but maintains the model’s output characteristics under standard sampling parameters.
Our approach is motivated by the fact that most open-weight models, such as Qwen (Bai et al., 2023) or Llama (Dubey et al., 2024), come with recommended stochastic sampling parameters (e.g. temperature $=0.7$ and $\textnormal{top}_{k}=20$ for Qwen3), under which even unquantized models’ outputs vary by a relative ${\sim}1\%$ on zero-shot or user evaluations.
The question we ask is: Is it possible to near-losslessly compress LLMs such that their outputs statistically match the original?

We examine this question along two complementary axes:

- •
*Task-lossless* (TL) quantization, where zero-shot benchmark accuracy is preserved within sampling variance.
- •
*Distribution-lossless* (DL) quantization, a stricter criterion where the quantized model’s output distribution is practically indistinguishable from the original.

Overall, we show that statistically lossless quantization matches realistic deployment scenarios while allowing significant gains beyond lossless compression and inference acceleration.
In addition, one key technical finding in the context of SLQ is the role of asymmetric quantization, i.e. the presence of a non-trivial zero-point parameter for the quantization grid, as a necessary design choice for achieving Distribution-lossless (DL) fidelity.

- •
We formalize the distinction between TL and DL compression for LLMs and propose the expected acceptance rate (EAR) metric, measuring the maximum token-agreement probability between original and quantized distributions under optimal coupling, as an interpretable output fidelity metric complementary to standard KL-divergence.
- •
We prove an error gap between symmetric and asymmetric quantization grids. Given a data distribution with values in the interval $[L,U]$, whose dynamic range $R=U-L$, and maximum value $M=\max(|L|,|U|)$, we define the *centering inefficiency* (“skewness”) relative to the grid as $\gamma=2M/R$. Under standard assumptions (Bennett, 1948), we prove a gamma-squared variance law for symmetric grids, saying that they inflate output variance by a factor of $\gamma^{2}$ relative to asymmetric quantization. We are the first to formally connect the asymmetric-vs-symmetric design choice to token-level decision flips, showing that asymmetric quantization greatly improves task-level accuracy.
- •
We present SLQ, a non-uniform scalar quantization pipeline using asymmetric quantization across bitwidths $\{2,3,4,5,6,7,8\}$, with multi-bitwidth Shapley sensitivity estimation and ILP-based allocation. For DL targets, the search operates directly on EAR constraints from calibration data. For TL targets, we introduce a single-point calibration procedure that exploits the linear KL-recovery relationship to derive bitwidth requirements from one benchmark measurement.
- •
From the accuracy side, we show that TL compression is achievable at high rates (as low as 3.3 bpp), while DL requires higher bit-widths of 5–6 bpp, corresponding to EAR $\geq 0.99$. We also show that SLQ can be supported efficiently on NVIDIA GPUs by leveraging the Humming kernel library (InclusionAI, 2025), for fast compressed inference across all integer bitwidths. We integrate our approach for end-to-end vLLM serving, yielding $1.7$–$3.6\times$ throughput over BF16, consistently outperforming FP8 while providing lossless accuracy.

Related Work.
Significant prior work focused on *weight-only* post-training quantization, from popular practical tooling (e.g., bitsandbytes (Dettmers et al., 2022)) to algorithmic methods such as GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023), AWQ (Lin et al., 2024), SqueezeLLM (Kim et al., 2024), SpQR (Dettmers et al., 2024), OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2023), HQQ (Badri and Shaji, 2023), QuIP and its follow-ups (Chee et al., 2023; Tseng et al., 2024a), additive/vector-quantization approaches (AQLM)(Egiazarian et al., 2024; Van Baalen et al., 2024), as well as more recent trellis-based methods such as QTIP (Tseng et al., 2024b), and YAQA (Tseng et al., 2025). A growing line of work targets *weight-and-activation* quantization via transformations and kernel support, e.g., QuaRot (Ashkboos and others, 2024) and SpinQuant (Liu et al., 2024).
Most techniques target fixed low-bit operating points (e.g., W4 or W4A4), and therefore primarily study the classic compression–accuracy trade-off rather than focusing on the compression achievable in the *near-lossless* regime which is the focus of this paper. Among prior work, only SpQR and YAQA explicitly consider this frontier; nevertheless, SpQR falls well short of the strict accuracy targets we consider, while YAQA relies on substantially heavier system support (e.g., full-model-KL-aware Hessian sketching and integration with specialized quantizers/kernels such as QTIP). In addition, as we show experimentally, YAQA still falls short of the distribution lossless (DL) objective.

There has also recently been work on non-uniform precision allocation for LLMs: for instance, HIGGS (Malinovskii et al., 2025) leverages linearity-style decompositions to push accuracy at low bitwidths; EvoPress (Sieberling et al., 2025) applies evolutionary search for dynamic compression; OWL applies layer-wise non-uniformity via outlier-guided layer-wise sparsity choices (Yin et al., 2023); and interaction-aware mixed-precision methods (e.g., IMPQ) incorporate cross-layer effects in allocation (Zhao et al., 2025).
Our work is complementary: rather than optimizing within the standard lossy region, we specifically focus on the *near-lossless* range where very small distributional shifts matter. We show for the first time that reaching this regime requires (i) asymmetric quantization and (ii) searching across a *wide* set of integer bitwidths rather than a small set of precisions studied by prior work.

A separate line of work targets *lossless* (bit-exact) compression of model weights. ZipNN (Hershcovitch et al., 2025) exploits redundancy in weight representations to achieve lossless compression and efficient (de)compression. Yet, it does not provide inference-time acceleration since weights must be decoded prior to execution: their method is designed to reduce weight storage. Yang et al. (2025) study exponent concentration and propose an entropy-coded *FP8*-centric representation (ECF8) with GPU-optimized decoding, reporting up to 26.9% memory savings and throughput improvements under fixed memory budgets; however, the approach is lossless with respect to an FP8 baseline and therefore applies most directly when models are stored/served in FP8. Finally, DFloat11 (Zhang et al., 2025) introduces dynamic-length, lossless compression of BF16 weights with online GPU decompression for inference under tight memory constraints. These works operate strictly in the lossless regime; for this, they require higher effective bitrates than our work.

## 2 Motivation

Figure: (a) Sampling variance of *uncompressed* Qwen3-8B.
Refer to caption: 2605.02404v1/assets/nongreedy_jitter.png

Most open-weight LLMs are deployed with stochastic decoding, so even *uncompressed* models exhibit measurable run-to-run variance on zero-shot evaluations (Figure [1](#S2.F1), left). A quantized model that stays within this natural variance is, for all practical purposes, lossless.
Figure [1(b)](#S2.F1.sf2) shows that in the near-lossless regime, zero-shot accuracy over standard tasks degrades approximately linearly with the KL divergence between the original and compressed models. This linearity means a single reference point is sufficient to anchor the compression search, without resorting to arbitrary KL thresholds; we formalize this as a single-point calibration procedure in Section [3.3](#S3.SS3).

Uniform quantization around 4 bits per parameter (bpp) has become the standard target for memory-constrained LLM deployment (Frantar et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024; Kurtić et al., 2025).
However, this target is arbitrary from an accuracy perspective: both GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023) and QTIP (Tseng et al., 2024b) incur significant KL divergence at ${\sim}$4 bpp on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), while our non-uniform allocation achieves $\text{KL}=0.004$ at 5.59 bpp (Table [4](#A3.T4), Appendix).
More generally, different models and layers have different sensitivities, so any fixed uniform bitwidth is either too aggressive or wasteful. Our approach inverts the problem: given a fidelity target, find the minimum non-uniform bitwidth that achieves it. We validate this across models in Section [4.1](#S4.SS1).

## 3 Method

We focus on obtaining near-lossless quantized models via layer-wise non-uniform scalar quantization, chosen for its broad support across GPUs (Frantar et al., 2024; 2023; Lin et al., 2024) and CPUs (Gerganov and llama.cpp contributors, 2023; Pegolotti et al., 2023; Ma and others, 2024); the approach should be valid for more complex quantized representations (e.g., vector quantization) as well.
Similarly, while we focus on GPTQ as our main quantization method, our approach could work with arbitrary other methods as well.
Non-uniform allocation is motivated by the observation that different layers exhibit different sensitivities to quantization (Dong et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2024). Given this target, we first show theoretically that asymmetric quantization is essential for distribution-lossless recovery (Section [3.1](#S3.SS1)). We will then describe the SLQ pipeline: output metrics (Section [3.2](#S3.SS2)), sensitivity estimation (Section [3.3](#S3.SS3)), and bitwidth allocation (Section [3.3](#S3.SS3)).

### 3.1 Asymmetric vs. Symmetric Quantization

Setup. Consider a weight tensor with values in $[L,U]$. Let $R=U-L$ denote the dynamic range, $M=\max(|L|,|U|)$ the maximum absolute magnitude, and $n$ the number of quantization levels. Symmetric quantization anchors the grid at zero, spanning $[-M,M]$ where $M=\max(|L|,|U|)$. Asymmetric quantization uses a zeropoint to shift the grid, spanning exactly $[L,U]$. Intuitively, symmetric quantization’s zero-anchored grid wastes representational capacity when weight distributions are offset from zero, yielding larger per-layer output errors that increase the probability of argmax changes. We provide an overview of the formalization below, showing the impact is *quadratic* in the centering inefficiency. The full argument is in Appendix [B](#A2).

###### Definition 3.1 (Centering inefficiency) .

The centering inefficiency is $\gamma=2M/R$.

When weights are symmetric about zero, $\gamma=1$. For asymmetric distributions (typical in LLMs), $\gamma>1$. For example, weights in $[-0.8,1.2]$ yield $\gamma=1.2$: the symmetric grid spans $[-1.2,1.2]$, wasting 20% of its capacity on the interval $[-1.2,-0.8]$ where no weights exist.

From step sizes to noise variance. Symmetric quantization has step size $\Delta_{\text{sym}}=2M/(n{-}1)$, while asymmetric has $\Delta_{\text{asym}}=R/(n{-}1)$. Their ratio is $\Delta_{\text{sym}}/\Delta_{\text{asym}}=2M/R=\gamma$, independent of the number of levels. Under the high-rate approximation (Bennett, 1948), quantization error is approximately uniform within each bin, with variance $\sigma^{2}=\Delta^{2}/12$. Since $\Delta_{\text{sym}}=\gamma\cdot\Delta_{\text{asym}}$, the variance inherits a *quadratic* dependence:

###### Lemma 3.2 (Gamma-Squared Variance Law) .

The quantization noise variance under symmetric quantization scales quadratically with the centering inefficiency relative to asymmetric quantization:

$$ $\sigma_{\mathrm{sym}}^{2}=\gamma^{2}\,\sigma_{\mathrm{asym}}^{2}.$ (1) $$

Impact on output error. For a single linear layer $\mathbf{y}=W\mathbf{x}$, quantization produces output perturbation $\bm{\delta}=(Q(W)-W)\mathbf{x}$, with expected squared norm $\sigma^{2}\|\mathbf{x}\|^{2}$. Since quantization is applied to identical inputs, $\|\mathbf{x}\|^{2}$ cancels out:

$$ $\frac{\mathbb{E}[\|\bm{\delta}_{\mathrm{sym}}\|^{2}]}{\mathbb{E}[\|\bm{\delta}_{\mathrm{asym}}\|^{2}]}=\gamma^{2}.$ (2) $$

This implies that a distribution with $\gamma=1.2$, which is only mildly asymmetric, suffers 44% more per-layer output error under symmetric quantization. This compounds across layers and increases the probability of *decision flips*: positions where quantization perturbs logits enough to change the argmax token, directly reducing EAR. The full derivation is in Appendix [B](#A2).

Empirical Validation.
We now validate the $\gamma^{2}$ scaling on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (uniform 4-bit). Figures [2](#S3.F2)–[3](#S3.F3) show symmetric quantization flips more tokens across all entropy bins, with the ratio largest at low entropy (up to $3\times$). The resulting EAR gap yields a full 1-bit efficiency advantage: asymmetric at 5.5 bits (EAR 0.972) exceeds symmetric at 6.5 bits (0.969). This gap persists even at 7.0 bits (0.985 vs. 0.974). Figure [4](#S3.F4) confirms that the effect compounds across layers: symmetric quantization degrades more sharply in the first few sensitive layers, undermining mixed-precision schemes. On MMLU-Pro, where each answer is a single-token decision, asymmetric quantization recovers 88.4% of FP16 accuracy versus 82.5% for symmetric (Appendix [C.2](#A3.SS2)).

### 3.2 Output Metrics for Losslessness

The non-uniform quantization problem is that of assigning a bitwidth $b_{\ell}\in\mathcal{B}=\{2,3,4,5,6,7,8\}$ to each layer $\ell$. The average bitwidth is $\bar{b}(\mathbf{b})=\sum_{\ell}b_{\ell}|W_{\ell}|/\sum_{\ell}|W_{\ell}|$. The first challenge is that of precise output metrics, computable from a small calibration set, that reliably predict when a configuration is near-lossless.

KL-Divergence. For $N$ calibration token positions with original and quantized distributions $p_{i}$, $q_{i}$, we compute $D_{\mathrm{KL}}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i}\sum_{k\in\mathcal{T}_{i}}p_{i}(k)\log\frac{p_{i}(k)}{q_{i}(k)}$, restricted to the top-$K{=}10$ tokens under $p_{i}$. However, KL divergence is measured in natural log units (nats), unbounded above, and asymmetric. Its magnitude is difficult to interpret without calibrated intuition for a given vocabulary and distribution shape. While Pinsker’s inequality ($d_{TV}\leq\sqrt{D_{\mathrm{KL}}/2}$) bounds KL into a percentage-like quantity, this bound is loose.

Expected Acceptance Rate (EAR). We therefore define the Expected Acceptance Rate (EAR) as the primary metric for *distribution-lossless* compression, inspired by the token acceptance probability in speculative decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023). In that setting, a draft token sampled from a proposal distribution $q$ is accepted under the target distribution $p$ with probability $\sum_{k}\min(p(k),q(k))$; the same quantity measures the fidelity of a quantized model’s output distribution to the original. Formally, this probability mass overlap equals $1-d_{TV}(p,q)$, is bounded in $[0,1]$, and is directly interpretable as a percentage of shared probability mass. By the coupling characterization of total variation distance, $\sum_{k}\min(p(k),q(k))$ equals the maximum probability that two random variables $X\sim p$ and $Y\sim q$ can be coupled to agree ($X=Y$). We average this over $N$ calibration positions:

$$ $\mathrm{EAR}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\min(p_{i}(k),q_{i}(k)),$ (3) $$

where both distributions are restricted to the top-$K$ tokens under $p_{i}$. EAR has a direct operational interpretation: $\text{EAR}=0.99$ means that, under optimal coupling, the original and quantized models produce the same token 99% of the time. While the underlying quantity $1-d_{TV}$ is classical, its application as a compression fidelity criterion for quantized LLMs is, to our knowledge, new.

Restricting to top-$K$ tokens is a tight approximation: LLM output distributions are sharply peaked, so the excluded tail contributes negligible mass. EAR is typically more restrictive than KL ($\text{EAR}\geq 0.99$ generally implies $D_{\mathrm{KL}}\ll 0.01$), and is robust to tail behavior since $\min(p,q)$ clips near-zero probabilities rather than producing unbounded penalties. We use EAR as the primary constraint for bitwidth search (Section [3.3](#S3.SS3)), with KL as a secondary diagnostic. Both are computed simultaneously during each forward pass at no additional cost.

### 3.3 Sensitivity Estimation and Bitwidth Allocation

Given the output metrics, we need to assign each layer a bitwidth from the budget $\mathcal{B}=\{2,3,4,5,6,7,8\}$. Prior mixed-precision work typically uses binary allocation (e.g., a layer is set to either 4 or 8 bits) (Zhao et al., 2025; Dong et al., 2019). We find this experimentally to be too coarse for the near-lossless regime: restricting to $\{4,8\}$ consistently requires higher average bitwidth than the full range to meet the same quality constraint (see Section [4.1](#S4.SS1)). Since inference engines often require certain layers to share a bitwidth (e.g., fused QKV projections in vLLM), we partition layers into $M$ groups $\mathcal{G}=\{G_{1},\ldots,G_{M}\}$.

Sensitivity Estimation.
We need to estimate how each group’s quantization at a given bitwidth affects the output metrics. A simple baseline is *linear estimation* (Malinovskii et al., 2025): model metric degradation as $\Delta_{\text{KL}}(\mathbf{b})\approx\sum_{m}\alpha_{m}\cdot e_{m}^{(b_{m})}$, where $e_{m}^{(b)}$ is the normalized reconstruction error and $\alpha_{m}$ is estimated via noise injection ($O(T\cdot M)$ forward passes). This is fast, but assumes independence across groups.

Multi-Bitwidth Shapley Estimation.
Shapley-based sensitivity estimation (Shapley, 1953) has been applied to mixed-precision quantization (Zhao et al., 2025), pruning (Adamczewski et al., 2024; Sun and others, 2025), and layer importance analysis (Zhang et al., 2024). However, existing approaches consider layer-wise binary choices, switching layers between one high and one low precision, which restricts allocation to a binary choice and would thus lead to higher average bitwidths (Section [4.1](#S4.SS1) and Appendix [D.1](#A4.SS1)).

We extend this to support the full bitwidth range by running a *separate* binary game for each target bitwidth $b^{*}\in\mathcal{B}\setminus\{b_{\text{max}}\}$. In each game, we sample $P$ random permutations, start with all groups at $b_{\text{max}}$, and sequentially switch each group to $b^{*}$, recording the marginal metric change. The Shapley value $\phi_{m}^{(b^{*})}$ for group $G_{m}$ is the average marginal contribution across permutations. Across all $|\mathcal{B}|-1$ games, this yields a full sensitivity database $\phi_{m}^{(b)}$ for every group-bitwidth pair. The games are independent and can run in parallel; each forward pass computes KL and EAR simultaneously. The total cost is $O(P\cdot M\cdot|\mathcal{B}|)$ forward passes. The full procedure is given in Algorithm [1](#alg1) (Appendix [A](#A1)).

Bitwidth Allocation.
Given the sensitivity database $c_{m,b}$ from either estimation method, we solve a multiple-choice knapsack problem via integer linear programming (ILP): assign each group $G_{m}$ a bitwidth $b$ to minimize total predicted degradation $\sum_{m,b}x_{m,b}\cdot c_{m,b}$ subject to a bitwidth budget. We then binary-search over the budget to find the minimum average bitwidth satisfying a quality constraint. The two compression targets use different constraints, described below. The same framework extends to joint weight-and-activation quantization by quantizing activations in the sensitivity estimates and allocation (Appendix [D.1](#A4.SS1)). Details on metric prediction and an alternative evolutionary search appear in Appendices [A.2](#A1.SS2) and [A.4](#A1.SS4). Appendix [C.1](#A3.SS1) visualizes the per-layer sensitivity landscape and resulting bitwidth assignments.

Distribution-lossless search. The sensitivity database directly predicts EAR and KL for any candidate configuration (Appendix [A.2](#A1.SS2)). We set a target such as $\text{EAR}\geq 0.99$ and binary-search over the bitwidth budget until the predicted EAR meets the constraint. The entire search operates on calibration-set metrics and requires no downstream benchmark evaluation.

Task-lossless search via single-point calibration. For task-lossless compression, the goal is to find the minimum bitwidth that preserves a target recovery rate (e.g., ${\geq}\,99\%$ of BF16 accuracy) across all benchmarks. We exploit the approximate linearity between KL divergence and task recovery established in Section [2](#S2) (Figure [1(b)](#S2.F1.sf2)):

$$ $\text{recovery}\approx 1-\alpha\cdot D_{\mathrm{KL}}.$ (4) $$

This linear regime holds reliably for the high-bitwidth configurations relevant to near-lossless compression, where the perturbation is small enough that accuracy degrades smoothly with distributional shift. We observe this consistently across six model families and five task categories (Appendix [C.4](#A3.SS4)): within the near-lossless range, recovery is well-approximated by a single slope, while at more aggressive compression the relationship becomes non-linear and task-dependent.

The intercept is known ($D_{\mathrm{KL}}{=}0$ implies $\text{recovery}{=}1$), so a single calibration point determines the slope $\alpha$. We choose a uniform bitwidth where the model shows meaningful degradation (e.g., uniform 4-bit), measure its actual KL divergence on calibration data, and evaluate benchmarks to obtain recovery. This yields $\alpha=(1-\text{recovery})/D_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\text{cal}}$, and inverting gives the KL threshold for any target recovery: $D_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\text{thresh}}=(1-\text{target recovery})/\alpha$.

Since Shapley-predicted KL may differ from measured KL by a systematic factor, we compute a calibration ratio $\rho=D_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\text{actual}}/D_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\text{predicted}}$ at the calibration point and apply it to all subsequent predictions: $\hat{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}=\rho\cdot D_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\text{predicted}}$. The binary search then checks $\hat{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}\leq D_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\text{thresh}}$ at each candidate bitwidth, requiring no additional forward passes. The total cost is one forward pass to measure KL plus two benchmark runs (BF16 baseline and calibration configuration); the search itself is arithmetic on the sensitivity database. The full procedure is given in Algorithm [2](#alg2) (Appendix [A.3](#A1.SS3)).

## 4 Experiments

We evaluate SLQ for weight-only quantization on four models: Qwen3-8B, Qwen3-32B, Qwen3.5-27B (Bai et al., 2023), and Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024).

Setup. Following our prior analysis, all models use asymmetric integer group quantization (group size 128, bitwidths $\{2,3,4,5,6,7,8\}$) with Shapley-based per-layer allocation (Section [3.3](#S3.SS3)) using 512 calibration samples; DL targets use $\text{EAR}\geq 0.985$–$0.99$ depending on the model. The uniform W4A16 baselines use GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023) with tuned hyperparameters; SLQ is orthogonal to the choice of per-layer quantizer and instead provides a principled method for finding the minimum-bitwidth non-uniform configuration that meets a given fidelity target. Quantized weights are served via Humming kernels (InclusionAI, 2025) (vLLM-integrated, MARLIN-based (Frantar et al., 2024), 4–8 bit; Appendix [C.3](#A3.SS3)). Zero-shot accuracy is measured on seven benchmarks: reasoning (ARC-Challenge, GSM8K, GSM8K-CoT), instruction following (IFEval), and knowledge (MMLU-CoT, MMLU-Pro), under non-greedy decoding with model-recommended sampling parameters, averaged over 3 seeds. End-to-end throughput is measured on NVIDIA L40s GPUs (vLLM + Humming kernels) using ShareGPT and Reasoning workloads; we report output tokens per second per GPU (TPS/GPU) to normalize across tensor-parallelism configurations.

## 5 Conclusion & Future Work

We formalized statistically-lossless LLM compression, distinguishing task-lossless (TL) and distribution-lossless (DL) targets, and proposed EAR as a fidelity metric. The $\gamma^{2}$ variance law shows asymmetric quantization is a prerequisite for DL compression. Our pipeline SLQ achieves TL at 3.3–4.7 bits, DL at 5.0–6.6 bits, and $1.7$–$3.6\times$ speedup over BF16 across four models. Future work includes further examination of weight-and-activation quantization (Appendix [D.1](#A4.SS1)) and speculative decoding.

### A.2 Metric Prediction from Sensitivity Data

During binary search, the predicted evaluation mode estimates KL divergence and EAR from sensitivity coefficients without running model inference. This enables rapid iteration over candidate bitwidth budgets.

#### Linear Method.

Under the independence assumption, total metric degradation is the sum of per-group contributions:

$$ $\displaystyle\widehat{\Delta D_{\text{KL}}}(\mathbf{b})$ $\displaystyle=\sum_{m}e_{m}^{(b_{m})}\cdot\alpha_{m}^{\text{KL}}$ (5) $\displaystyle\widehat{\Delta\text{EAR}}(\mathbf{b})$ $\displaystyle=\sum_{m}e_{m}^{(b_{m})}\cdot\alpha_{m}^{\text{EAR}}$ (6) $$

where $e_{m}^{(b)}$ is the normalized reconstruction error for group $G_{m}$ at bitwidth $b$, computed as $e_{m}^{(b)}=\frac{1}{|G_{m}|}\sum_{\ell\in G_{m}}\|W_{\ell}-\hat{W}_{\ell}^{(b)}\|_{F}^{2}/\|W_{\ell}\|_{F}^{2}$. The coefficients $\alpha_{m}^{\text{KL}}$ and $\alpha_{m}^{\text{EAR}}$ are estimated via noise injection as described in Section [3.3](#S3.SS3). The predicted metrics are then $\widehat{D_{\text{KL}}}=\widehat{\Delta D_{\text{KL}}}$ and $\widehat{\text{EAR}}=1-\widehat{\Delta\text{EAR}}$.

#### Shapley Method.

The Shapley values $\phi_{m}^{(b)}$ directly represent expected metric degradation from assigning group $m$ to bitwidth $b$ relative to the reference bitwidth $b_{\max}$:

$$ $\displaystyle\widehat{D_{\text{KL}}}(\mathbf{b})$ $\displaystyle=\sum_{m}\phi_{m}^{\text{KL},(b_{m})}$ (7) $\displaystyle\widehat{\text{EAR}}(\mathbf{b})$ $\displaystyle=1-\sum_{m}\phi_{m}^{\text{EAR},(b_{m})}$ (8) $$

Since Shapley values are computed using actual quantized weights during permutation sampling, they capture both reconstruction error magnitude and its propagation through the network. For unified permutation sampling, which outputs transition costs rather than absolute costs, we apply cumulative summation from $b_{\max}$ downward before use in the solver.

### A.4 Evolutionary Search as an Alternative Solver

As an alternative to the ILP-based solver (Section [3.3](#S3.SS3)), bitwidth allocation can be formulated as a combinatorial search problem. We implement a variant of EvoPress (Sieberling et al., 2025) adapted to our constraint-based setting: whereas the original minimizes loss subject to a bitwidth budget, we minimize bitwidth subject to a quality constraint. Algorithm [3](#alg3) summarizes the procedure.

Figure: Algorithm 3 Constraint-Based Evolutionary Bitwidth Search

#### Fitness Function.

For an offspring $\mathbf{b}^{\prime}$ with parent $\mathbf{b}$, let $\Delta b=\bar{b}(\mathbf{b})-\bar{b}(\mathbf{b}^{\prime})$ denote the bitwidth reduction. When $\Delta b>0$, fitness is:

$$ $f(\mathbf{b}^{\prime})=\frac{D_{\text{KL}}(\mathbf{b}^{\prime})}{\Delta b}\cdot\begin{cases}1&\text{if }D_{\text{KL}}(\mathbf{b}^{\prime})\leq\tau\\ \gamma\cdot(D_{\text{KL}}(\mathbf{b}^{\prime})-\tau)&\text{otherwise}\end{cases}$ (9) $$

where $\gamma\gg 1$ is a penalty coefficient. Lower fitness is better. Offspring that increase bitwidth ($\Delta b<0$) receive heavily penalized fitness values.

#### Adaptive Curation.

The inverted formulation introduces a challenge: because the search only accepts bitwidth-reducing mutations, suboptimal early allocations cannot be corrected. When progress stalls, curation performs bitwidth-neutral swaps between equal-sized groups, keeping the average constant while reallocating capacity from insensitive to sensitive groups. This allows the search to escape local optima and continue compressing.

#### Limitations.

The evolutionary search requires orders of magnitude more forward passes than the ILP-based solver and does not scale to large models. We include it here as a reference formulation; all experimental results in the main paper use the sensitivity-based ILP solver.

## Appendix B Derivation of the Gamma-Squared Variance Law

This appendix provides a self-contained derivation of Lemma [3.2](#S3.Thmtheorem2). We proceed in three steps: analyzing quantizer step sizes, deriving quantization noise variance via Bennett’s high-rate approximation, and combining the two results.

### B.1 Definitions and Preliminaries

Let $W$ be a scalar random variable representing a neural network weight, with probability density function supported on $[L,U]$ where $L<U$. We recall the geometric quantities from Definition [3.1](#S3.Thmtheorem1): the dynamic range $R=U-L$, the maximum absolute magnitude $M=\max(|L|,|U|)$, and the centering inefficiency $\gamma=2M/R$. For distributions that are not symmetric about zero, the symmetric interval $[-M,M]$ strictly contains $[L,U]$, so $\gamma>1$.

### B.2 Step 1: Quantization Step Size Analysis

Consider a uniform scalar quantizer with $n$ discrete levels (typically $n=2^{b}$ for bitwidth $b$). The step size $\Delta$ is the distance between adjacent quantization levels.

#### Asymmetric quantizer.

The grid spans exactly $[L,U]$, dividing the range $R$ into $n-1$ intervals:

$$ $\Delta_{\mathrm{asym}}=\frac{R}{n-1}.$ (10) $$

#### Symmetric quantizer.

The grid is constrained to be symmetric about zero, spanning $[-M,M]$:

$$ $\Delta_{\mathrm{sym}}=\frac{2M}{n-1}.$ (11) $$

###### Lemma B.1 (Step size scaling) .

The symmetric step size relates to the asymmetric step size by $\Delta_{\mathrm{sym}}=\gamma\,\Delta_{\mathrm{asym}}$.

###### Proof.

Direct computation:

$$ $\frac{\Delta_{\mathrm{sym}}}{\Delta_{\mathrm{asym}}}=\frac{2M/(n-1)}{R/(n-1)}=\frac{2M}{R}=\gamma.$ (12) $$

∎

###### Lemma B.1 (Step size scaling) .

###### Proof.

### B.3 Step 2: Quantization Noise Variance

We analyze the noise characteristics using classical results from quantization theory.

#### Bennett’s approximation vs. Bussgang decomposition.

Two classical results characterize quantization noise. Bennett’s approximation (Bennett, 1948) shows that, under the high-rate assumption (many quantization levels), the quantization error $e=Q(W)-W$ is approximately uniformly distributed within each bin, $e\sim\mathcal{U}[-\Delta/2,\,\Delta/2]$. The only requirement is that the probability density of $W$ is approximately constant within each bin, which is satisfied when the number of quantization levels is large relative to the variation in the density. The Bussgang decomposition (Bussgang, 1952) provides a complementary signal-processing perspective, decomposing the quantizer output as $Q(W)=\alpha W+d$ where $\alpha$ is a gain term and $d$ is noise uncorrelated with the input ($\mathbb{E}[Wd]=0$). This uncorrelation guarantee, however, requires the input $W$ to be Gaussian. Since LLM weight distributions are generally non-Gaussian, we rely on Bennett’s result, which applies under the weaker high-rate condition.

###### Lemma B.2 (Variance of quantization noise) .

Under the high-rate assumption, the variance of the quantization noise is $\sigma^{2}=\Delta^{2}/12$.

###### Proof.

The mean of $e\sim\mathcal{U}[-\Delta/2,\,\Delta/2]$ is zero. The variance is:

$$ $\displaystyle\sigma^{2}$ $\displaystyle=\int_{-\Delta/2}^{\Delta/2}x^{2}\cdot\frac{1}{\Delta}\,dx$ (13) $\displaystyle=\frac{1}{\Delta}\left[\frac{x^{3}}{3}\right]_{-\Delta/2}^{\Delta/2}$ (14) $\displaystyle=\frac{1}{\Delta}\cdot\frac{2(\Delta/2)^{3}}{3}$ (15) $\displaystyle=\frac{\Delta^{2}}{12}.$ (16) $$

∎

###### Lemma B.2 (Variance of quantization noise) .

###### Proof.

### B.4 Step 3: Proof of the Gamma-Squared Law

We combine Lemma [B.1](#A2.Thmtheorem1) and Lemma [B.2](#A2.Thmtheorem2) to establish the main result.

###### Proof of Lemma 3.2 .

Applying Lemma [B.2](#A2.Thmtheorem2) to each quantizer:

$$ $\sigma_{\mathrm{asym}}^{2}=\frac{\Delta_{\mathrm{asym}}^{2}}{12},\qquad\sigma_{\mathrm{sym}}^{2}=\frac{\Delta_{\mathrm{sym}}^{2}}{12}.$ (17) $$

Substituting $\Delta_{\mathrm{sym}}=\gamma\,\Delta_{\mathrm{asym}}$ from Lemma [B.1](#A2.Thmtheorem1):

$$ $\displaystyle\sigma_{\mathrm{sym}}^{2}$ $\displaystyle=\frac{(\gamma\,\Delta_{\mathrm{asym}})^{2}}{12}$ (18) $\displaystyle=\gamma^{2}\cdot\frac{\Delta_{\mathrm{asym}}^{2}}{12}$ (19) $\displaystyle=\gamma^{2}\,\sigma_{\mathrm{asym}}^{2}.$ (20) $$

∎

The geometric penalty $\gamma$ imposed by symmetric quantization on asymmetric weight distributions therefore leads to a quadratic increase in quantization noise variance. For a single linear layer $\mathbf{y}=W\mathbf{x}$, the output perturbation due to quantization is $\bm{\delta}=E\mathbf{x}$ where $E=Q(W)-W$. The expected squared error per output unit is $\sigma^{2}\|\mathbf{x}\|^{2}$. Since symmetric and asymmetric quantization are applied to the same weights with identical inputs, $\|\mathbf{x}\|^{2}$ cancels in the ratio, giving $\mathbb{E}[\|\bm{\delta}_{\mathrm{sym}}\|^{2}]/\mathbb{E}[\|\bm{\delta}_{\mathrm{asym}}\|^{2}]=\gamma^{2}$. This provides the analytical foundation for the empirical observations in Section [3.2](#S3.Thmtheorem2), where symmetric quantization exhibits consistently higher flip rates and lower EAR.

### C.4 KL Divergence and Zero-Shot Performance

To establish the relationship between output fidelity and downstream task performance, we conduct controlled experiments that systematically vary the degree of distributional shift from the original model. We inject uniform noise into model weights at increasing levels, where noise magnitude is scaled by each weight matrix’s standard deviation to approximate quantization error characteristics. For each noise level, we compute KL divergence between the perturbed and original model’s output distributions, then evaluate zero-shot accuracy across five task categories: Reasoning (ARC, HellaSwag, WinoGrande, PIQA), Knowledge (MMLU, TriviaQA), Math (GSM8K, MATH), Code (HumanEval, MBPP), and Retrieval (NeedleBench).

Figure [8](#A3.F8) presents degradation curves across six models spanning different scales and architectures. The results reveal a consistent pattern: accuracy remains stable while KL divergence remains small, then degrades as divergence increases. The specific onset of degradation varies with model scale and task category, with Retrieval tasks demonstrating the greatest robustness and Math and Code proving more sensitive to distributional shift. Crucially, all models maintain baseline accuracy when KL divergence stays below 0.01. This establishes the link between distribution-lossless (DL) and task-lossless (TL) compression: a model that is DL (high distributional fidelity, as measured by EAR or KL) is also TL (preserves zero-shot task accuracy). The converse does not hold: a model can be TL at a lower bitwidth without meeting the stricter DL criterion, since benchmark scores are less sensitive than distributional metrics (Section [3.3](#S3.SS3)).

### C.5 Token-Level Divergence Analysis

To visualize the practical impact of distribution-lossless (DL) compression,
we perform a token-level comparison between the base Qwen3-8B (FP16)
and the SLQ-DL quantized model (5.70 bpw, EAR $=99\%$).
We generate responses with the base model using greedy decoding,
then teacher-force the identical token sequence through the EAR model
and record every position where the EAR model’s greedy next-token prediction differs.
Divergent tokens appear
highlighted as base
with the EAR model’s alternative shown as a ^blue superscript.

#### Methodology.

The goal of this analysis is to measure how often the quantized model
would choose a different token than the base model at each generation step,
i.e. how frequently quantization causes the model to “start diverging”
from the original output.
To measure this cleanly, we use *teacher-forced comparison*:
we first generate a full response from the base model using greedy decoding,
then feed that exact token sequence as input to the EAR model
and record, at every position, whether the EAR model’s greedy top-1 prediction
matches the base model’s token.
Crucially, the EAR model is always conditioned on the base model’s actual tokens,
not its own previous predictions.
This is necessary because in free (autoregressive) generation,
a single divergent token shifts the entire continuation,
making it impossible to attribute later differences to quantization error
versus the natural consequence of conditioning on a different prefix.
Teacher-forcing isolates the per-position effect of quantization:
each reported divergence reflects a position where quantization alone
caused the model to prefer a different token, independent of any
earlier divergences.

#### General statistics.

We evaluate 15 diverse prompts spanning code generation, mathematical derivation,
essay writing, logic puzzles, and everyday questions, producing 6,531 tokens in total.
Of these, 6,407 tokens (98.1%) are identical between both models;
only 124 tokens (1.9%) diverge.
Per-prompt agreement ranges from 96.1% to 99.6%
with a median of 98.2%.
Divergences are distributed roughly uniformly across token positions
(mean position 235 out of 512),
confirming that quantization errors do not compound over longer sequences.
All observed divergences are minor stylistic variations—synonym swaps,
formatting changes, or punctuation differences—and none alter factual content.

### D.1 Detailed Quantization Results

This section presents comprehensive quantization results across bitwidths, number formats, and quantization schemes. All results are evaluated on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct using 512 calibration samples. The metrics reported here (EAR, KL, Margin, PPL) are distributional fidelity measures used for distribution-lossless (DL) compression. Task-lossless (TL) compression targets benchmark recovery instead and can be achieved at lower bitwidths; see the main results in Section [4.1](#S4.SS1).

#### Metrics.

We report four key metrics for quantization quality:

- •
EAR: The expected acceptance rate, measuring the overlap in output probability mass between BF16 and quantized models. This equals $\sum_{k}\min(p_{k},q_{k})$ where $p$ and $q$ are the top-10 token distributions. Higher EAR indicates closer alignment to the original output distribution.
- •
Margin at Disagreement: The average probability gap on tokens where the quantized and BF16 models disagree on their top prediction. Lower margin means flips occur only on close calls, indicating smaller quantization error.
- •
KL Divergence: The KL divergence between BF16 and quantized output distributions on the calibration set.
- •
Perplexity (PPL): The perplexity of the quantized model relative to BF16, computed from the calibration set.

#### Format and Bitwidth Notations.

All tables use asymmetric integer quantization (Asym) or symmetric integer quantization (Sym) with group size 128. Bitwidth values (e.g., “4.50”) include the overhead from scales and zero-points. The format notation “4-8” means only bitwidths 4 and 8 are available for allocation, while “4-5-6-7-8” means all intermediate bitwidths are available.
