## Abstract

Abstract Many physical AI tasks are governed by implicit equilibrium: an agent actuates a subset of degrees of freedom (boundary DoFs), while the remaining free DoFs settle by minimizing a total potential energy. Even seemingly basic tasks such as bending a deformable linear object (DLO) to a target shape can exhibit strongly nonlinear behavior due to multi-stability: the same boundary conditions may yield multiple equilibrium shapes depending on the actuation trajectory. However, learning and control in such systems is brittle because the actuation-to-configuration map is defined only implicitly, and naive backpropagation through iterative equilibrium solvers is memory- and compute-intensive. We propose Neural Control, a boundary-control framework that computes trajectory-dependent, memory-efficient proxy gradients by differentiating equilibrium conditions via an adjoint formulation, avoiding unrolling of solver iterations. To improve robustness over long horizons, we integrate these sensitivities into a receding-horizon MPC scheme that repeatedly re-anchors optimization to realized equilibria and mitigates basin-switching in multi-stable regimes. We evaluate Neural Control in simulation and on physical robots manipulating DLOs, and show improved performance over gradient-free baselines such as SPSA and CEM.

## 1 Introduction

Many physical systems encountered in robotics, graphics, and simulation are governed by an energy functional, with system states defined implicitly as solutions of energy minimization problems under boundary and loading conditions. In the absence of non-conservative forces, controlling such systems amounts to shaping the energy landscape – by adjusting boundary conditions or parameters – so that the resulting equilibrium achieves a desired objective. This setting poses a fundamental challenge: equilibria are high-dimensional and often multi-stable, with small changes in boundary actuation inducing discontinuous transitions between solution branches.

These difficulties are especially pronounced in robotic manipulation of deformable structures (Figure [1](#S1.F1)A), such as deformable linear objects (DLOs) (Mora et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024), where large, geometrically nonlinear deformations and hysteresis lead to multi-stable equilibria and path-dependent behavior (Qiao et al., 2020; Liang et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2025; Bretl and McCarthy, 2014; Tong et al., 2021). Figure [1](#S1.F1)B illustrates this phenomenon, showing that a DLO can admit multiple stable equilibrium shapes under identical boundary conditions. A fundamental challenge is that state updates are defined only implicitly as the solution of equilibrium problems – such as energy minimization – over high-dimensional discretizations of continua (Yin et al., 2021; Moll and Kavraki, 2006; Bergou et al., 2008). As a result, this equilibrium map can be history-dependent due to basin-of-attraction effects, breaking standard Markov assumptions and making long-horizon planning and learning brittle (Hausknecht and Stone, 2015; Suh et al., 2022).

Differentiable simulation offers gradients for optimization (Qiao et al., 2020; Mora et al., 2021; Suh et al., 2022), but multistable control requires branch-consistent, trajectory-dependent sensitivities that follow the realized branch selected by the solver (Georgiev et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024). Markovian transition models are inherently single-valued; in multi-stable systems, identical states and actions can converge to different equilibria, causing learned dynamics to average over branches unless latent modes are modeled explicitly (Bishop, 1994; Hafner et al., 2019b, a). Moreover, when each step requires an implicit equilibrium solve, backpropagating through solver iterations over long horizons is memory-intensive and numerically fragile near instabilities (Gruslys et al., 2016; Amos and Kolter, 2017; Bai et al., 2019).

This motivates a method that computes trajectory-dependent sensitivities through sequences of implicit equilibrium solves without unrolling inner iterations, while remaining robust to branch selection over long horizons.
Inspired by adjoint sensitivity methods in continuous-depth learning (Chen et al., 2018),
we introduce an adjoint-based framework that computes memory-efficient, trajectory-dependent proxy gradients by differentiating equilibrium conditions, while keeping the forward rollout on converged equilibria along the realized branch.
To improve robustness in multi-stable regimes, we embed these sensitivities into a receding-horizon model predictive control (MPC) loop (Chua et al., 2018) that repeatedly replans from realized configurations and mitigates basin drift over long continuations.
We validate the approach on shape control of DLOs, with simulation and real-robot experiments on elastic strips under noise, modeling mismatch, and gravity.
Compared to gradient-free baselines (SPSA (Spall, 2002) and CEM (Botev et al., 2013)),
Adjoint+RHC is 19–127$\times$ faster and achieves 1–6 orders of magnitude lower best loss across all elastic strip manipulation tasks (Table [1](#S4.T1)).

This paper makes the following contributions:
(i) an adjoint formulation that computes trajectory-dependent gradients by implicit differentiation of equilibrium conditions, enabling long-horizon optimization through sequences of equilibrium solves without unrolling inner Newton/CG iterations;
(ii) a receding-horizon continuation (RHC) scheme that repeatedly re-anchors optimization to realized equilibria and mitigates unintended basin switches in multi-stable regimes;
(iii) simulation and real-robot demonstrations on DLOs, including transfer under parameter mismatch and gravity.

## 2 Related works

#### Optimization and learning for long-horizon, path-dependent control.

Long-horizon control and sequential decision-making are central themes in machine learning, where performance is often limited by delayed credit assignment and partial observability, and, in model-based settings, by compounding model error.
A common response is to cast history dependence as a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) and augment policies and value functions with memory, e.g., recurrent reinforcement learning (RL) (Hausknecht and Stone, 2015), or to learn latent state-space models that support planning via imagined rollouts (Hafner et al., 2019b, a).
Another dominant paradigm is model-based RL with receding-horizon planning: actions are optimized over a short horizon and repeatedly replanned using model predictive control (MPC) to reduce error accumulation and improve robustness under regime changes (Chua et al., 2018).
Complementary to these approaches, black-box and sampling-based optimizers remain strong baselines for long-horizon problems because they avoid differentiating through complex transitions and can explore multi-modal outcomes.
Representative examples include simultaneous perturbation stochastic approximation (SPSA) (Spall, 2002), the cross-entropy method (CEM) (Botev et al., 2013), and parameter-space search methods such as evolution strategies (ES) (Salimans et al., 2017) and augmented random search (ARS) (Mania et al., 2018). Recent work also makes CEM differentiable (DCEM) (Amos and Yarats, 2020).
However, the rollout cost of these methods typically grows quickly with horizon length and action dimension, which becomes especially acute when each step involves an expensive implicit equilibrium computation (Pinneri et al., 2021).
Our method retains receding-horizon replanning but computes physics-informed, trajectory-level sensitivities through implicit transitions along the realized branch, enabling efficient gradient-based optimization.

#### Memory-efficient, trajectory-based gradients for long horizons.

Backpropagation through time (BPTT) provides a direct way to obtain long-horizon, trajectory-dependent gradients by propagating sensitivities across timesteps (Williams and Peng, 1990), but naively storing intermediate states makes memory scale with horizon length, motivating checkpointing and recomputation strategies (Gruslys et al., 2016).
In differentiable physics and control, this challenge becomes more pronounced when each transition is an implicit update solved via inner iterations (e.g., Newton or conjugate gradient), since differentiating through solver iterations is memory-intensive and can be numerically brittle near instabilities (Amos and Kolter, 2017; Suh et al., 2022).
Inspired by adjoint sensitivity methods (Chen et al., 2018), a complementary line of work avoids unrolling by differentiating through implicit functions via optimality or fixed-point conditions, including deep equilibrium models (Bai et al., 2019) and differentiable implicit layers (Look et al., 2020).
Related ideas also appear in differentiable control, such as differentiable MPC via KKT conditions (Amos et al., 2018) and Pontryagin differentiable programming (PDP) (Jin et al., 2020).
In contrast to DEQ/implicit layers that differentiate a single fixed point, we address long-horizon optimization through *sequences* of equilibria where multi-stability makes the realized trajectory branch-dependent.
In contrast to differentiable MPC/PDP, our setting requires sensitivities through an implicit equilibrium solve at every step rather than explicit dynamics or a single KKT system.
Compared to BPTT through solver iterations, we avoid unrolling inner solves and use a proxy-adjoint recursion; we further adopt a frozen-tangent proxy to avoid higher-order derivatives along long horizons.

#### Differentiable models for learning and control.

Differentiable modeling and simulation have become core primitives for gradient-based learning, planning, and inverse problems, and recent ML toolchains such as DiffTaichi (Hu et al., 2019a) and Brax (Freeman et al., 2021) make optimizing through physics practical at scale.
Differentiable simulators have been extended from rigid bodies to deformables and soft bodies, including ChainQueen (Hu et al., 2019b), DiffPD (Du et al., 2021), and differentiable soft multi-body simulation (Qiao et al., 2021).
For flexible structures, discrete differential geometry (DDG) discretizations provide geometry-preserving, energy-based models with analytic derivatives (Bergou et al., 2008; Grinspun et al., 2003; Huang et al., 2025).
Benchmarks further clarify the difficulty of long-horizon deformable manipulation, e.g., SoftGym (Lin et al., 2021) and PlasticineLab (Huang et al., 2021).
Learned differentiable simulators can offer fast predictors, but near multistability and bifurcation boundaries deterministic models can blur branch selection without explicit multimodality (e.g., mixture formulations) (Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020; Pfaff et al., 2021; Bishop, 1994).
Recent work also integrates analytic gradients from differentiable multiphysics simulations into RL updates (e.g., SAPO) (Xing et al., 2025).
Our work complements these efforts by focusing on the gradient pathway itself: memory-efficient, trajectory-level sensitivities through sequences of implicit equilibrium solves so optimization follows the realized branch.

## 3 Methodology

We study learning and control problems where the system state is defined implicitly by an equilibrium condition rather than an explicit dynamics model. A continuation parameter $\lambda\in[0,1]$ indexes the process, with $\lambda=0$ the initial configuration and $\lambda=1$ the terminal configuration. At each $\lambda$, the equilibrium state $\mathbf{x}(\lambda)$ is obtained by solving

$$ $\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x}(\lambda),\mathbf{z}(\lambda))=\mathbf{0},$ (1) $$

where $\mathbf{z}(\lambda)$ denotes controllable quantities entering the equilibrium (e.g., boundary conditions, loads, or design parameters), rather than by an explicit state update of the form $\mathbf{x}_{k+1}=f(\mathbf{x}_{k})$. In mechanics, Equation ([1](#S3.E1)) corresponds to Newton’s second law in the quasi-static regime, where inertia and vibrational effects are neglected and internal conservative forces balance applied loads. A common instance is

$$ $\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z})=\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}E(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z})-\mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{ext}},$ (2) $$

where $E$ is total potential energy and $\mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{ext}}$ collects external loads.

Equilibrium-governed systems pose two challenges: (i) $\mathbf{x}(\lambda)$ is typically computed by iterative solvers (e.g., Newton–CG), so differentiating through solver iterations across long horizons is expensive; and (ii) equilibria can be multi-stable, so for fixed $\mathbf{z}$, multiple stable solutions may exist and warm-started solvers follow a realized branch through their basins of attraction. We therefore generate rollouts by warm-started equilibrium solves along the continuation path.

A neural controller parameterized by $\Theta$ produces a continuation control signal

$$ $\mathbf{u}(\lambda)=\mathbf{u}_{\Theta}(\lambda),$ (3) $$

which drives $\mathbf{z}(\lambda)$ via known continuation dynamics

$$ $\frac{d\mathbf{z}}{d\lambda}=f(\mathbf{z}(\lambda),\mathbf{u}(\lambda)),\qquad\mathbf{z}(0)=\mathbf{z}_{0}.$ (4) $$

Given $\mathbf{z}(\lambda)$, the realized equilibrium $\mathbf{x}(\lambda)$ is obtained by repeatedly solving ([1](#S3.E1)) with warm starts from the previous solution, yielding a branch-consistent rollout.
In all experiments, the forward rollout is exact in the sense that each state is a converged equilibrium.

### 3.1 Adjoint gradients via proxy sensitivity dynamics

At any converged equilibrium $(\mathbf{x}(\lambda),\mathbf{z}(\lambda))$ satisfying $\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z})=\mathbf{0}$,
implicit differentiation yields the local equilibrium sensitivity

$$ $\mathbf{S}(\lambda):=\frac{\partial\mathbf{x}}{\partial\mathbf{z}}(\lambda),\qquad\mathbf{G}_{x}(\lambda)\,\mathbf{S}(\lambda)=-\mathbf{G}_{z}(\lambda),$ (5) $$

where $\mathbf{G}_{x}=\frac{\partial\mathcal{G}}{\partial\mathbf{x}}$ and $\mathbf{G}_{z}=\frac{\partial\mathcal{G}}{\partial\mathbf{z}}$.
We never form $\mathbf{S}(\lambda)$; instead we compute products $\mathbf{S}^{T}(\lambda)\mathbf{v}$ via one adjoint linear solve with $\mathbf{G}_{x}(\lambda)$.

Along a smooth realized branch, the total derivative satisfies

$$ $\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{d\lambda}=\mathbf{S}(\lambda)\frac{d\mathbf{z}}{d\lambda}+\frac{\partial\mathbf{x}}{\partial\lambda}.$ (6) $$

In our instantiation, $\mathcal{G}$ has no explicit $\lambda$-dependence: any scheduling is absorbed into $\mathbf{z}(\lambda)$.
Consequently, $\partial\mathbf{x}/\partial\lambda=\mathbf{0}$ and

$$ $\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{d\lambda}=\mathbf{S}(\lambda)\frac{d\mathbf{z}}{d\lambda}.$ (7) $$

Combining ([7](#S3.E7)) with the continuation dynamics ([4](#S3.E4)) gives the continuous sensitivity identity

$$ $\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{d\lambda}=\mathbf{S}(\lambda)\,f(\mathbf{z}(\lambda),\mathbf{u}(\lambda)).$ (8) $$

Equation ([8](#S3.E8)) defines the proxy sensitivity dynamics used only for backward sensitivity propagation;
the forward states $\mathbf{x}(\lambda)$ are still obtained by solving ([1](#S3.E1)) to convergence at each
continuation step. All gradients below are derived as adjoints of this proxy sensitivity dynamics, combined with implicit
differentiation at converged equilibria.

The only approximation in our gradient construction is a *frozen-tangent* discretization: we evaluate $\mathbf{S}(\lambda)$ on the realized rollout at discrete continuation steps and treat it as locally constant during the backward pass (equivalently, we ignore $d\mathbf{S}/d\lambda$). This avoids higher-order derivatives of Eq. ([1](#S3.E1)). Appendix [C](#A3) formalizes this approximation and shows that, under mild regularity assumptions on the realized equilibrium branch, the resulting proxy gradient is first-order accurate.

#### Terminal objective.

We first consider a terminal loss

$$ $\mathcal{L}=\phi(\mathbf{x}(1),\mathbf{z}(1)).$ (9) $$

Define proxy-system costates $\mathbf{a}(\lambda):=\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\mathbf{x}(\lambda)$ and
$\mathbf{g}(\lambda):=\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\mathbf{z}(\lambda)$.
Under ([8](#S3.E8)) and frozen tangent, the terminal conditions are

$$ $\displaystyle\mathbf{a}(1)$ $\displaystyle=\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\phi(\mathbf{x}(1),\mathbf{z}(1)),$ (10) $\displaystyle\mathbf{g}(1)$ $\displaystyle=\nabla_{\mathbf{z}}\phi(\mathbf{x}(1),\mathbf{z}(1))+\mathbf{S}(1)^{\top}\mathbf{a}(1),$ $$

where $\mathbf{S}(1)^{\top}\mathbf{a}(1)$ accounts for the dependence of the terminal equilibrium on $\mathbf{z}(1)$.

Let $\mathbf{A}(\lambda):=\frac{\partial f}{\partial\mathbf{z}}(\mathbf{z}(\lambda),\mathbf{u}(\lambda))$ and $\mathbf{B}(\lambda):=\frac{\partial f}{\partial\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{z}(\lambda),\mathbf{u}(\lambda))$. Under the frozen-tangent approximation, we neglect the dependence of $\mathbf{S}(\lambda)$ on $\mathbf{x}$, yielding zero local Jacobian of the proxy dynamics with respect to $\mathbf{x}$. The costates satisfy backward ODEs integrated from $\lambda=1$ to 0,

$$ $\displaystyle-\frac{d\mathbf{a}}{d\lambda}$ $\displaystyle=\mathbf{0},$ (11) $\displaystyle-\frac{d\mathbf{g}}{d\lambda}$ $\displaystyle=\mathbf{A}(\lambda)^{\top}\!\Big(\mathbf{g}(\lambda)+\mathbf{S}(\lambda)^{\top}\mathbf{a}(\lambda)\Big),$ $$

and the instantaneous control gradient is

$$ $\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\mathbf{u}(\lambda)}=\mathbf{B}(\lambda)^{\top}\!\Big(\mathbf{g}(\lambda)+\mathbf{S}(\lambda)^{\top}\mathbf{a}(\lambda)\Big).$ (12) $$

The parameter gradient follows by the chain rule

$$ $\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\Theta}=\int_{0}^{1}\left(\frac{\partial\mathbf{u}_{\Theta}(\lambda)}{\partial\Theta}\right)^{\top}\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\mathbf{u}(\lambda)}\,d\lambda.$ (13) $$

### 3.2 Adjoints for trajectory objectives

Many equilibrium-control tasks specify objectives along the realized branch rather than only at $\lambda=1$.
We therefore consider

$$ $\mathcal{L}=\phi(\mathbf{x}(1),\mathbf{z}(1))+\int_{0}^{1}w(\lambda)\,\ell(\mathbf{x}(\lambda),\mathbf{z}(\lambda))\,d\lambda,$ (14) $$

where $w(\lambda)\geq 0$ is user-specified.
The terminal conditions remain ([10](#S3.E10)).
Under ([8](#S3.E8)) and frozen tangent, the backward ODEs become

$$ $-\frac{d\mathbf{a}}{d\lambda}=w(\lambda)\,\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\ell(\mathbf{x}(\lambda),\mathbf{z}(\lambda)),$ (15) $$

$$ $\displaystyle-\frac{d\mathbf{g}}{d\lambda}=$ $\displaystyle w(\lambda)\Big(\nabla_{\mathbf{z}}\ell(\mathbf{x}(\lambda),\mathbf{z}(\lambda))+\mathbf{S}(\lambda)^{\top}\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\ell(\mathbf{x}(\lambda),\mathbf{z}(\lambda))\Big)$ (16) $\displaystyle+\mathbf{A}(\lambda)^{\top}\!\Big(\mathbf{g}(\lambda)+\mathbf{S}(\lambda)^{\top}\mathbf{a}(\lambda)\Big),$ $$

and the control/parameter gradients retain the same form as
Eqs. ([12](#S3.E12))–([13](#S3.E13)).

### 3.3 Receding-horizon continuation control

Over long continuations, proxy/discretization errors can accumulate, and in multi-stable regions overly aggressive updates may drive the equilibrium solve across basins of attraction.
We therefore solve the task as a sequence of short continuation segments (MPC-style), repeatedly re-anchoring optimization to realized equilibria.

At segment index $m$, starting from $(\mathbf{x}^{(m)}_{0},\mathbf{z}^{(m)}_{0})$, we optimize a control schedule over a local homotopy $\lambda\in[0,1]$ (implemented with $H$ continuation steps of size $1/H$).
The continuation control is generated by a lightweight neural controller

$$ $\mathbf{u}(\lambda)=\mathbf{u}_{\Theta^{(m)}}(\lambda),$ (17) $$

with fixed architecture and parameters $\Theta^{(m)}$ *reinitialized* at the start of each segment and optimized
within the segment. We minimize a short-horizon segment objective $\mathcal{L}^{(m)}$ of the same form as
Eq. ([14](#S3.E14)), restricted to the segment rollout:

$$ $\displaystyle\min_{\Theta^{(m)}}\;\mathcal{L}^{(m)}=$ $\displaystyle\phi(\mathbf{x}^{(m)}(1),\mathbf{z}^{(m)}(1))$ (18) $\displaystyle+$ $\displaystyle\int_{0}^{1}w(\lambda)\,\ell(\mathbf{x}^{(m)}(\lambda),\mathbf{z}^{(m)}(\lambda))\,d\lambda.$ $$

subject to the equilibrium constraint ([1](#S3.E1)), continuation dynamics ([4](#S3.E4)), and bounded progress (e.g., $\|\mathbf{u}(\lambda)\|\leq u_{\max}$).
We optimize $\Theta^{(m)}$ using the proxy-adjoint gradients above and stop early when the segment objective stalls (or after a fixed budget).
Executing the segment yields $(\mathbf{x}^{(m)}(1),\mathbf{z}^{(m)}(1))$, and we shift the horizon by

$$ $\mathbf{x}^{(m+1)}_{0}\leftarrow\mathbf{x}^{(m)}(1),\qquad\mathbf{z}^{(m+1)}_{0}\leftarrow\mathbf{z}^{(m)}(1).$ (19) $$

This segment-wise design localizes proxy error and reduces unintended basin switches in multi-stable landscapes.
We provide the discrete derivation, implementation details, and pseudocode in Appendix [A](#A1) and Algorithm [1](#alg1).

## 4 Results

We evaluate our method on elastic-strip manipulation tasks (Figure [1](#S1.F1)A).
The strip undergoes large deformations and admits multi-stable equilibria, inducing strong path dependence: small continuation errors can accumulate and trigger undesired switches between equilibrium branches.
We consider three tasks that progressively stress these effects: (i) point-to-point manipulation (terminal objective),
(ii) path following of a designated strip point (trajectory objective), and (iii) shape formation in multi-stable regimes (branch selection).

All methods share the same forward rollout, which computes strip configurations by solving implicit equilibria to convergence at each continuation step.
We compare our full method (Adjoint+RHC) against SPSA and CEM, and ablate our approach by removing RHC (Adjoint-only).
Each method is run for the same number of parameter-update steps $T$ (optimizer updates on $\Theta$).
An objective evaluation corresponds to one full rollout that computes the task loss from implicit equilibrium solves.
Because SPSA uses two rollouts per update (two-sided perturbations) and CEM uses $P$ rollouts per update (population evaluation), these gradient-free methods consume multiple objective evaluations per update.
Adjoint-only and Adjoint+RHC use one rollout per update and additionally solve tangent/adjoint linear systems to obtain gradients, avoiding backpropagation through the Newton/CG iterations of the equilibrium solver.
We therefore report both best achieved loss and total wall-clock time, reflecting rollout cost and gradient overhead.
Finally, we validate the approach in real-world robotic experiments.

### 4.1 Experimental setup

We model the manipulated object as a planar elastic strip with stretching and bending energy.
At each continuation step, the forward rollout computes the realized configuration by solving the corresponding
equilibrium equations to convergence under imposed boundary constraints.
We partition degrees of freedom into boundary nodes $\mathbf{q}_{b}$ and free nodes $\mathbf{q}_{f}$ and set
$\mathbf{z}\equiv\mathbf{q}_{b}$, $\mathbf{x}\equiv\mathbf{q}_{f}$ (Figure [1](#S1.F1)B): control acts only through boundary
conditions, while free nodes are solved implicitly and used to evaluate task losses.
The strip is held by clamped boundary conditions at both ends; Tasks 1–2 actuate transverse (global x-axis in our setup) displacements at both ends,
and Task 3 actuates $(x,y)$ displacement and in-plane rotation at one end with the other end passively clamped.
Unless otherwise stated, we discretize the strip with $N=101$ nodes and ignore gravity in numerical experiments.
The stretching and bending stiffness are fixed to $k_{s}=0.31~\mathrm{N}$ and $k_{b}=7.85\times 10^{-8}~\mathrm{N\,m^{2}}$.
Full discretization and solver details are provided in the Appendix [B](#A2).

### 4.2 Tasks and metrics

We evaluate three numerical tasks that progressively test terminal objectives, trajectory objectives, and multi-stable shape
(branch) selection. In all tasks, boundary degrees of freedom define the controllable variable $\mathbf{z}\equiv\mathbf{q}_{b}$,
and the free degrees of freedom $\mathbf{x}\equiv\mathbf{q}_{f}$ are solved implicitly from equilibrium and used to evaluate losses.
Tasks 1–2 start from the same pre-deformed configuration (Figure [2](#S4.F2)A), while Task 3 starts from a straight
strip (Figure [3](#S4.F3)).

The continuation control is produced by a neural controller $\mathbf{u}(\lambda)=\mathbf{u}_{\Theta}(\lambda)$, where
$\mathbf{u}(\lambda)$ specifies the rate of boundary actuation along continuation.
In Tasks 1–2, $\mathbf{u}(\lambda)\in\mathbb{R}^{2}$ outputs the transverse ($x$-direction) displacement rates of the left and
right clamped ends. In Task 3, $\mathbf{u}(\lambda)\in\mathbb{R}^{3}$ outputs the $(x,y)$ displacement rates and in-plane
rotation rate of the manipulated end, while the other end remains passively clamped.

Task 1: point-to-point node targeting.
We select a strip node $i$ and a target position $\mathbf{p}_{i}^{\star}$ within the reachable workspace.
The goal is to drive the node to the target at the end of continuation. We use the terminal loss

$$ $\mathcal{L}_{\text{pos}}=\frac{1}{2}\big\|\mathbf{p}_{i}(1)-\mathbf{p}_{i}^{\star}\big\|_{2}^{2},$ (20) $$

and declare success if $\mathcal{L}_{\text{pos}}\leq\varepsilon_{\text{pos}}$.

Task 2: trajectory tracking.
We track a prescribed planar path with a designated node (the middle node). Let $\mathbf{p}^{\star}_{\mathrm{mid}}(\lambda)$
denote the reference trajectory (sinusoid, circle, or square wave; Fig. [2](#S4.F2)B). We define

$$ $\ell_{\text{traj}}(\lambda)=\frac{1}{2}\big\|\mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{mid}}(\lambda)-\mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{mid}}^{\star}(\lambda)\big\|_{2}^{2},$ (21) $$

and evaluate the trajectory objective

$$ $\mathcal{L}_{\text{traj}}=\int_{0}^{1}\ell_{\text{traj}}(\lambda)\,d\lambda,$ (22) $$

implemented as a Riemann sum over realized continuation steps (and, for RHC, over the concatenated executed trajectory).
We declare success if $\mathcal{L}_{\text{traj}}\leq\varepsilon_{\text{traj}}$.

Task 3: shape formation under multi-stability.
We shape the strip by manipulating one clamped end while the other end remains passively clamped. For fixed boundary
conditions, multiple stable equilibria may coexist; given a target shape, the goal is to reach the corresponding
equilibrium branch. We measure shape discrepancy by curvature matching:

$$ $\mathcal{L}_{\kappa}=\int_{0}^{L}\big(\kappa(s;1)-\kappa^{\star}(s)\big)^{2}\,ds,$ (23) $$

where $\kappa^{\star}(s)$ is the target curvature. We declare success if $\mathcal{L}_{\kappa}\leq\varepsilon_{\kappa}$.

### 4.4 Baselines and ablations

We compare four optimizers for elastic-strip manipulation: (i) SPSA, (ii) CEM, (iii) our neural continuation control without RHC (Adjoint-only; full-horizon), and (iv) our full method, which combines the same
adjoint-based neural control with RHC (Adjoint+RHC). All methods share the same forward simulator (implicit equilibrium solves to convergence at each continuation step) and the same controller parameterization $\mathbf{u}_{\Theta}(\lambda)$ with $\lambda$ as the sole input; they differ only in how the controller parameters $\Theta$ are updated and whether receding-horizon execution is used.

## 5 Discussion

Our results highlight two recurring difficulties in equilibrium-governed control: (i) obtaining usable gradients when states are defined only implicitly by a converged equilibrium solve, and (ii) maintaining branch consistency over long continuations in multi-stable landscapes. We address (i) by keeping the forward pass exact (each rollout state is a converged equilibrium) while computing proxy-adjoint gradients that avoid differentiating through Newton–CG iterations. Despite the frozen-tangent approximation, these gradients are sufficiently informative to optimize terminal and trajectory objectives. We address (ii) with RHC, which localizes proxy/discretization error to short segments and repeatedly re-anchors optimization at realized equilibria. This wrapper is most beneficial in multi-stable regimes, where it reduces basin drift and improves reliability of branch selection.

Limitations include frozen-tangent bias (ignored $d\mathbf{S}/d\lambda$ and higher-order terms), reduced reliability near bifurcations or under non-smooth physics, and limited lookahead in segment-wise updates, which may require stronger terminal objectives or shaping to solve long-horizon tasks. Equilibrium solves remain the dominant cost, motivating better warm-starting, preconditioning, and adaptive step sizing.

## 6 Conclusion

We presented proxy-adjoint neural continuation for control and learning in equilibrium-defined systems, combining exact equilibrium rollouts with efficient implicit sensitivities and a receding-horizon continuation (RHC) wrapper for robust long-horizon branch tracking. Empirically, Adjoint+RHC achieves large efficiency gains: it is 19–127$\times$ faster than SPSA/CEM across Tasks 1–3 while reaching 3–6 orders of magnitude lower best loss on the same fixed update budgets. Finally, we demonstrate qualitative sim-to-real transfer on a 6-DoF robotic setup under gravity and experimentally identified material parameters (Figure [5](#S4.F5)), indicating that proxy-adjoint continuation is a practical tool for real manipulation of compliant, multi-stable structures.

## Appendix A Discrete proxy-adjoint derivation and implementation details

This appendix derives the discrete proxy-adjoint recursions used in Section [3.1](#S3.SS1) and shows how we compute policy gradients without differentiating through the equilibrium solver. The key primitive is a matrix-free routine for products $\mathbf{S}_{k}^{\top}\mathbf{v}$, where $\mathbf{S}_{k}=\partial\mathbf{x}_{k}/\partial\mathbf{z}_{k}$ is the equilibrium sensitivity at step $k$.

#### Forward rollout and notation.

We discretize $\lambda\in[0,1]$ into $\{\lambda_{k}\}_{k=0}^{K}$ with step size $\Delta\lambda=1/K$.
At each step $k=0,\ldots,K-1$, the controller outputs $\mathbf{u}_{k}=\mathbf{u}_{\Theta}(\lambda_{k})$ and the continuation variable updates explicitly as

$$ $\mathbf{z}_{k+1}=\mathbf{z}_{k}+\Delta\lambda\,f(\mathbf{z}_{k},\mathbf{u}_{k}).$ (24) $$

The state $\mathbf{x}_{k}$ is defined as the converged equilibrium at $(\mathbf{z}_{k},\lambda_{k})$, warm-started from the previous solution:

$$ $\displaystyle\mathbf{x}_{k}=\mathrm{SolveEq}(\mathbf{z}_{k},\lambda_{k};\ \mathrm{init}=\mathbf{x}_{k-1}),$ (25) $\displaystyle\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x}_{k},\mathbf{z}_{k},\lambda_{k})=\mathbf{0}.$ $$

Along the realized rollout, define the equilibrium Jacobians

$$ $\mathbf{G}_{x,k}:=\left.\frac{\partial\mathcal{G}}{\partial\mathbf{x}}\right|_{(\mathbf{x}_{k},\mathbf{z}_{k},\lambda_{k})},\qquad\mathbf{G}_{z,k}:=\left.\frac{\partial\mathcal{G}}{\partial\mathbf{z}}\right|_{(\mathbf{x}_{k},\mathbf{z}_{k},\lambda_{k})},$ (26) $$

and the continuation-dynamics Jacobians

$$ $\mathbf{A}_{k}:=\left.\frac{\partial f}{\partial\mathbf{z}}\right|_{(\mathbf{z}_{k},\mathbf{u}_{k})},\qquad\mathbf{B}_{k}:=\left.\frac{\partial f}{\partial\mathbf{u}}\right|_{(\mathbf{z}_{k},\mathbf{u}_{k})}.$ (27) $$

#### Implicit sensitivity and 𝐒 k ⊤ ​ 𝐯 \mathbf{S}_{k}^{\top}\mathbf{v} products.

Let $\mathbf{S}_{k}:=\partial\mathbf{x}_{k}/\partial\mathbf{z}_{k}$ denote the local sensitivity of the equilibrium map at step $k$.
Implicit differentiation of $\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x}_{k},\mathbf{z}_{k},\lambda_{k})=\mathbf{0}$ yields

$$ $\mathbf{S}_{k}:=\frac{\partial\mathbf{x}_{k}}{\partial\mathbf{z}_{k}},\qquad\mathbf{G}_{x,k}\mathbf{S}_{k}=-\mathbf{G}_{z,k}.$ (28) $$

We never form $\mathbf{S}_{k}$. Instead, for any vector $\mathbf{v}$ we compute $\mathbf{S}_{k}^{\top}\mathbf{v}$ via one adjoint linear solve:

$$ $\text{solve}\quad\mathbf{G}_{x,k}^{\top}\mathbf{p}=\mathbf{v},\qquad\text{then}\quad\mathbf{S}_{k}^{\top}\mathbf{v}=-\,\mathbf{G}_{z,k}^{\top}\mathbf{p}.$ (29) $$

This matrix-free product is the only place where equilibrium sensitivities enter the gradient computation.

#### Proxy dynamics and frozen tangent

To backpropagate without differentiating through $\mathrm{SolveEq}$, we use the proxy identity
$d\mathbf{x}/d\lambda\approx\mathbf{S}(\lambda)\,f(\mathbf{z}(\lambda),\mathbf{u}(\lambda))$,
discretized as the proxy transition

$$ $\mathbf{x}_{k+1}\approx\mathbf{x}_{k}+\Delta\lambda\,\mathbf{S}_{k}\,f(\mathbf{z}_{k},\mathbf{u}_{k}).$ (30) $$

The forward pass still uses exact equilibria ([25](#A1.E25)); ([30](#A1.E30)) is used *only* to define a tractable backward pass.
We further apply a frozen-tangent approximation: $\mathbf{S}_{k}$ (equivalently $\mathbf{G}_{x,k},\mathbf{G}_{z,k}$ on the realized rollout) is treated as constant and we drop terms involving $d\mathbf{S}/d\lambda$ and higher-order derivatives. Receding-horizon continuation limits the accumulation of this proxy mismatch by restricting the adjoint propagation horizon (Section [3.3](#S3.SS3)).

#### Objective, proxy adjoints, and gradients

We write terminal and trajectory objectives in one form:

$$ $\mathcal{L}=\phi(\mathbf{x}_{K},\mathbf{z}_{K})+\sum_{k=0}^{K-1}w_{k}\,\ell(\mathbf{x}_{k},\mathbf{z}_{k}),$ (31) $$

where $\phi$ is an optional terminal loss and $\ell$ is an optional per-step loss (terminal-only uses $w_{k}\equiv 0$; trajectory-only uses $\phi\equiv 0$).

Define adjoints $\mathbf{a}_{k}:=\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\mathbf{x}_{k}$ and $\mathbf{g}_{k}:=\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\mathbf{z}_{k}$.
At $k=K$,

$$ $\mathbf{a}_{K}=\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\phi(\mathbf{x}_{K},\mathbf{z}_{K}),\qquad\mathbf{g}_{K}=\nabla_{\mathbf{z}}\phi(\mathbf{x}_{K},\mathbf{z}_{K})+\mathbf{S}_{K}^{\top}\mathbf{a}_{K},$ (32) $$

where $\mathbf{S}_{K}^{\top}\mathbf{a}_{K}$ is computed using ([29](#A1.E29)).
From the explicit continuation update ([24](#A1.E24)),

$$ $\frac{\partial\mathbf{z}_{k+1}}{\partial\mathbf{z}_{k}}=\mathbf{I}+\Delta\lambda\,\mathbf{A}_{k},\qquad\frac{\partial\mathbf{z}_{k+1}}{\partial\mathbf{u}_{k}}=\Delta\lambda\,\mathbf{B}_{k}.$ (33) $$

Under the proxy transition ([30](#A1.E30)) and frozen tangent, the proxy adjoints propagate for $k=K-1,\ldots,0$ as

$$ $\mathbf{a}_{k}\approx\mathbf{a}_{k+1}+w_{k}\,\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\ell(\mathbf{x}_{k},\mathbf{z}_{k}),$ (34) $$

$$ $\displaystyle\mathbf{g}_{k}\approx$ $\displaystyle(\mathbf{I}+\Delta\lambda\,\mathbf{A}_{k})^{\top}\mathbf{g}_{k+1}+\Delta\lambda\,\mathbf{A}_{k}^{\top}(\mathbf{S}_{k}^{\top}\mathbf{a}_{k+1})$ (35) $\displaystyle+w_{k}\,\nabla_{\mathbf{z}}\ell(\mathbf{x}_{k},\mathbf{z}_{k})+w_{k}\,\mathbf{S}_{k}^{\top}\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\ell(\mathbf{x}_{k},\mathbf{z}_{k}),$ $$

where each $\mathbf{S}_{k}^{\top}(\cdot)$ is evaluated by ([29](#A1.E29)).

For convenience define

$$ $\mathbf{d}_{k}:=\mathbf{g}_{k+1}+\mathbf{S}_{k}^{\top}\mathbf{a}_{k+1}.$ $$

Then the control gradient at each step is

$$ $\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\mathbf{u}_{k}}\approx\Delta\lambda\,\mathbf{B}_{k}^{\top}\mathbf{d}_{k},$ (36) $$

and the policy-parameter gradient follows by the chain rule through the controller:

$$ $\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\Theta}=\sum_{k=0}^{K-1}\left(\frac{\partial\mathbf{u}_{\Theta}(\lambda_{k})}{\partial\Theta}\right)^{\top}\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\mathbf{u}_{k}}.$ (37) $$

## Appendix B Elastic strip equilibrium model and implicit solver ( SolveEq )

This section instantiates the equilibrium residual $\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z},\lambda)$ and the implicit solver
$\mathbf{x}_{k}=\mathrm{SolveEq}(\mathbf{z}_{k},\lambda_{k})$ in Eq. ([25](#A1.E25)) for our elastic strip simulator.

#### State and controls.

Figure: Figure 6: Discrete schematic of an elastic strip showing the discretization and notation: node positions $\{\mathbf{q}_{i}\}$, segment tangents $\{\mathbf{t}_{i}\}$, and turning angles $\{\theta_{i}\}$.
Refer to caption: 2605.03288v1/x5.png

As shown in Fig. [6](#A2.F6), we discretize the strip centerline into $N{+}1$ nodes
$\{\mathbf{q}_{i}\}_{i=0}^{N}$ with $\mathbf{q}_{i}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$ ($d{=}2$ or $3$), and stack them as
$\mathbf{q}=[\mathbf{q}_{0}^{\top},\ldots,\mathbf{q}_{N}^{\top}]^{\top}$.
We clamp two nodes at each end and treat them as boundary (controllable) DoFs,

$$ $\displaystyle\mathbf{q}_{b}$ $\displaystyle=[\mathbf{q}_{0}^{\top},\mathbf{q}_{1}^{\top},\mathbf{q}_{N-1}^{\top},\mathbf{q}_{N}^{\top}]^{\top},$ (38) $\displaystyle\mathbf{q}_{f}$ $\displaystyle=[\mathbf{q}_{2}^{\top},\ldots,\mathbf{q}_{N-2}^{\top}]^{\top}.$ $$

We identify $\mathbf{x}\equiv\mathbf{q}_{f}$ and $\mathbf{z}\equiv\mathbf{q}_{b}$.

#### Energy model.

Strip equilibrium is defined by a stationary point of the total potential energy

$$ $E_{\mathrm{tot}}(\mathbf{q}_{f})=E_{s}(\mathbf{q}_{f},\mathbf{q}_{b})+E_{b}(\mathbf{q}_{f},\mathbf{q}_{b})+E_{g}(\mathbf{q}_{f},\mathbf{q}_{b}),$ (39) $$

including stretching, bending, and gravity.(^1^11We do not model twist; the strip is represented by its centerline with stretching and bending energies.)

#### Stretching energy.

Let $\Delta l$ be the undeformed edge length and $\mathbf{e}_{i}:=\mathbf{q}_{i+1}-\mathbf{q}_{i}$ for $i=0,\ldots,N-1$.
We use

$$ $E_{s}=\frac{1}{2}k_{s}\sum_{i=0}^{N-1}\left(1-\frac{\|\mathbf{e}_{i}\|}{\Delta l}\right)^{2}\Delta l.$ (40) $$

#### Bending energy.

Let $\phi_{i}$ be the turning angle at node $i$ (Fig. [6](#A2.F6)(d)) and $\phi_{i}^{0}$ the rest angle (zero in our study).
We use

$$ $E_{b}=\frac{1}{2}k_{b}\sum_{i=2}^{N-1}\left(2\tan\frac{\phi_{i}}{2}-2\tan\frac{\phi_{i}^{0}}{2}\right)^{2}\frac{1}{\Delta l}.$ (41) $$

#### Gravitational energy.

We use a lumped-mass gravity potential

$$ $E_{g}=-\sum_{i=0}^{N}m_{i}\,\mathbf{g}^{\top}\mathbf{q}_{i},$ (42) $$

where $\mathbf{g}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$ is the gravitational acceleration and $m_{i}$ is the lumped nodal mass.

#### Equilibrium residual.

Given $\mathbf{q}_{b}$, the equilibrium configuration solves

$$ $\displaystyle\mathbf{q}_{f}^{\star}(\mathbf{q}_{b})=\arg\min_{\mathbf{q}_{f}}E_{\mathrm{tot}}(\mathbf{q}_{f},\mathbf{q}_{b})$ (43) $$

We therefore define

$$ $\displaystyle\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z})=\nabla_{\mathbf{q}_{f}}E_{\mathrm{tot}}(\mathbf{q}_{f},\mathbf{q}_{b}),$ (44) with $\displaystyle\mathbf{x}\equiv\mathbf{q}_{f},\ \mathbf{z}\equiv\mathbf{q}_{b}.$ $$

The Jacobians $\mathbf{G}_{x,k}$ and $\mathbf{G}_{z,k}$ in Eq. ([26](#A1.E26)) are the corresponding derivatives of
$\mathcal{G}$ evaluated at $(\mathbf{q}_{f,k},\mathbf{q}_{b,k})$.

#### Implicit solver ( SolveEq ).

We implement $\mathbf{x}_{k}=\mathrm{SolveEq}(\mathbf{z}_{k},\lambda_{k})$ using a trust-region Newton–CG method applied to
Eq. ([43](#A2.E43)). At iterate $\mathbf{q}_{f}^{(t)}$, we assemble
$E_{\mathrm{tot}}^{(t)}$, $\mathbf{g}^{(t)}:=\nabla_{\mathbf{q}_{f}}E_{\mathrm{tot}}$, and
$\mathbf{H}^{(t)}:=\nabla_{\mathbf{q}_{f}}^{2}E_{\mathrm{tot}}$, and symmetrize
$\mathbf{H}^{(t)}\leftarrow\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{H}^{(t)}+\mathbf{H}^{(t)\top})$.

#### Trust-region step.

We approximately solve

$$ $\min_{\|\mathbf{p}\|_{2}\leq\Delta^{(t)}}\;m^{(t)}(\mathbf{p}):=\mathbf{g}^{(t)\top}\mathbf{p}+\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{p}^{\top}\mathbf{H}^{(t)}\mathbf{p}.$ (45) $$

If $\mathbf{H}^{(t)}$ is SPD (via Cholesky-type factorization), we compute the Newton step
$\mathbf{H}^{(t)}\mathbf{p}_{N}^{(t)}=-\mathbf{g}^{(t)}$ and take $\mathbf{p}^{(t)}=\mathbf{p}_{N}^{(t)}$ if feasible;
otherwise we take a standard dogleg boundary step between the Cauchy and Newton directions.
If SPD detection fails, we use Steihaug PCG with Hessian-vector products, terminating on negative curvature
$\mathbf{d}^{\top}\mathbf{H}^{(t)}\mathbf{d}\leq 0$ or on hitting $\|\mathbf{p}\|_{2}=\Delta^{(t)}$.
We use a Jacobi preconditioner
$\mathbf{M}^{(t)}=\mathrm{diag}(|\mathrm{diag}(\mathbf{H}^{(t)})|)+\sigma\mathbf{I}$.

#### Acceptance, radius update, and termination.

Let $\mathrm{pred}^{(t)}=-m^{(t)}(\mathbf{p}^{(t)})$ and
$\mathrm{ared}^{(t)}=E^{(t)}-E_{\mathrm{tot}}(\mathbf{q}_{f}^{(t)}+\mathbf{p}^{(t)},\mathbf{q}_{b};\lambda)$, and define
$\rho^{(t)}=\mathrm{ared}^{(t)}/\max(\mathrm{pred}^{(t)},\epsilon)$.
We accept the step if $\rho^{(t)}\geq\eta$, and update $\Delta^{(t)}$ using standard shrink/expand rules based on $\rho^{(t)}$.
We terminate when $\|\mathbf{g}^{(t)}\|_{2}\leq\varepsilon_{g}$, $\Delta^{(t)}\leq\varepsilon_{\Delta}$, or after a maximum number of iterations.
We warm-start continuation step $k$ with the previous equilibrium $\mathbf{q}_{f,k}^{(0)}\leftarrow\mathbf{q}_{f,k-1}^{\star}$;
under multistability, this initialization selects a specific locally stable equilibrium branch for the same $\mathbf{z}$.

## Appendix C Assumptions and consistency of the frozen-tangent proxy gradient

We formalize when the frozen-tangent proxy used in Sec. [3.1](#S3.SS1) yields a controlled approximation to the exact trajectory gradient along the realized equilibrium branch.

We consider the equilibrium constraint (cf. ([1](#S3.E1)))

$$ $\mathcal{G}(\mathbf{x}(\lambda),\mathbf{z}(\lambda))=0,\qquad\lambda\in[0,1],$ (46) $$

where $\mathbf{z}(\lambda)$ is induced by the continuation dynamics $d\mathbf{z}/d\lambda=f(\mathbf{z}(\lambda),\mathbf{u}(\lambda))$.
Write $G_{x}:=\partial\mathcal{G}/\partial\mathbf{x}$, $G_{z}:=\partial\mathcal{G}/\partial\mathbf{z}$, and $G_{\lambda}:=\partial\mathcal{G}/\partial\lambda$.

###### Assumption C.1 (Smooth stable realized branch) .

$\mathcal{G}$ is $C^{2}$ in $(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z})$ and $C^{1}$ in $\lambda$ in a neighborhood of the realized rollout.
The warm-started solver follows a unique realized branch $\mathbf{x}(\lambda)\in C^{1}([0,1])$, and $G_{x}(\lambda)$ is nonsingular for all $\lambda\in[0,1]$.
This assumption excludes points where the realized equilibrium loses stability or switches basins (e.g., near bifurcations), where $G_{x}$ may become singular or ill-conditioned.

###### Assumption C.1 (Smooth stable realized branch) .

#### Existence and sensitivity.

Assumption [C.1](#A3.Thmtheorem1) implies (by the Implicit Function Theorem) that locally $\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{x}^{\star}(\mathbf{z},\lambda)$ and the equilibrium sensitivity
$\mathbf{S}(\lambda):=\partial\mathbf{x}/\partial\mathbf{z}$ exists and satisfies

$$ $G_{x}(\lambda)\,\mathbf{S}(\lambda)=-G_{z}(\lambda),\qquad\mathbf{S}(\lambda)=-G_{x}(\lambda)^{-1}G_{z}(\lambda).$ (47) $$

#### Exact vs. proxy sensitivity dynamics.

Differentiating ([46](#A3.E46)) along $\lambda$ gives the exact identity

$$ $\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{d\lambda}=\mathbf{S}(\lambda)\,\frac{d\mathbf{z}}{d\lambda}\;-\;G_{x}(\lambda)^{-1}G_{\lambda}(\lambda).$ (48) $$

When explicit $\lambda$-dependence is absent or absorbed into $\mathbf{z}(\lambda)$ (as in Sec. [3.1](#S3.SS1)), $G_{\lambda}\equiv 0$ and

$$ $\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{d\lambda}=\mathbf{S}(\lambda)\,f(\mathbf{z}(\lambda),\mathbf{u}(\lambda)).$ (49) $$

Our proxy adjoint uses the same forward equilibria $\mathbf{x}(\lambda)$ from solving ([1](#S3.E1)) but freezes the tangent in the backward pass, i.e., it neglects the contribution of $d\mathbf{S}/d\lambda$ when propagating sensitivities through the sequence.

###### Assumption C.2 (Slowly varying tangent) .

Along the realized branch,

$$ $\sup_{\lambda\in[0,1]}\left\|\frac{d}{d\lambda}\mathbf{S}(\lambda)\right\|\leq\varepsilon,$ (50) $$

for some $\varepsilon>0$.
In the conservative case $\mathcal{G}=\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}E(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z})-F^{\mathrm{ext}}(\lambda)$, this holds when the tangent stiffness
$H(\lambda)=\nabla^{2}_{\mathbf{x}\mathbf{x}}E(\mathbf{x}(\lambda),\mathbf{z}(\lambda))$ is uniformly positive definite and the third derivatives of $E$ are bounded along the rollout.
On a discretization $\lambda_{k}$ with step size $\Delta\lambda$, ([50](#A3.E50)) implies $\|\mathbf{S}(\lambda_{k+1})-\mathbf{S}(\lambda_{k})\|\leq\varepsilon\,\Delta\lambda$ by the mean value theorem.

All norms are induced operator norms unless otherwise stated.

###### Theorem C.3 (First-order consistency of frozen-tangent proxy gradient) .

Consider the trajectory objective ([14](#S3.E14)) and the proxy-gradient construction in Secs. [3.1](#S3.SS1)–[3.2](#S3.SS2) (freezing $\mathbf{S}(\lambda)$ in the backward pass). Under Assumptions [C.1](#A3.Thmtheorem1)–[C.2](#A3.Thmtheorem2), the proxy gradient is first-order accurate:

$$ $\big\|\nabla_{\Theta}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{proxy}}-\nabla_{\Theta}\mathcal{L}\big\|\;\leq\;C\,\varepsilon,$ (51) $$

where $C$ depends on bounds of $f$, $\partial f/\partial(\mathbf{z},\mathbf{u})$, $(G_{x}^{-1},G_{z})$, and the objective derivatives along the realized branch, but not on $\varepsilon$.

Remark (role of receding horizon).
RHC limits the accumulation of proxy error by shortening the horizon over which the frozen-tangent approximation is applied and re-anchoring optimization to realized equilibria after each segment, which is especially helpful near basin boundaries where $\|d\mathbf{S}/d\lambda\|$ may increase.
When basin switching occurs or $G_{x}$ becomes ill-conditioned, the bound in ([51](#A3.E51)) need not hold; empirically, RHC reduces these events by keeping segments local and reinitializing from realized equilibria.

and we set $\mathbf{g}=\mathbf{0}$ (no gravity) in all numerical experiments.

At each continuation step we solve for the free DoFs using a trust-region Newton–CG method (App. [B](#A2)),
warm-started from the previous equilibrium.
We use initial radius $\Delta_{0}=10^{-3}$, acceptance threshold $\eta=0.1$, shrink/expand factors $(0.5,2.0)$, and at most $200$ outer iterations.
We terminate when $\|\nabla E_{\texttt{tot}}\|\leq 10^{-9}$ (gradient w.r.t. free DoFs) or when the radius drops below $10^{-8}$.

If the Hessian is detected SPD we take a Newton/dogleg step; otherwise we use Steihaug PCG in the trust region.
PCG is capped at $200$ iterations and stops when the PCG residual satisfies
$\|r\|\leq\min(0.5,\sqrt{\|\nabla E\|})\|\nabla E\|$, on negative curvature, or upon hitting the trust-region boundary.
We use a diagonal (Jacobi) preconditioner.

#### Controller parameterization and optimization.

We parameterize the continuation controller $u_{\Theta}(\lambda)$ as a lightweight MLP that takes the scalar $\lambda\in[0,1]$ as input and outputs the control vector $u(\lambda)\in\mathbb{R}^{d_{u}}$.
For Tasks 1–2, $d_{u}=2$ (left/right transverse displacement rates); for Task 3, $d_{u}=3$ (end-effector $(x,y)$ displacement rates and in-plane rotation rate).
Unless otherwise stated, we use $2$–$3$ hidden layers with width $64$ (architectures $[64,64]$ or $[64,64,64]$), ReLU activations, and a final $\tanh$ to bound the output; we scale the output to enforce $\|u(\lambda)\|\leq u_{\max}$.

For the adjoint-only baseline, we use a single rollout with full horizon $K=101$ continuation steps.
For RHC+adjoint, we use segment horizon $H=10$ continuation steps per segment (with horizon shifting as described in Sec. [3.3](#S3.SS3)).
All numerical experiments use Adam with learning rate $10^{-2}$ to optimize $\Theta$, and we keep the optimizer settings identical across methods.

#### Baseline hyperparameters.

We use population size $N_{\mathrm{pop}}=10$, elite fraction $0.3$, smoothing $\alpha=0.25$, and standard deviation settings $\sigma_{0}=0.5\,u_{\max}$, with a minimum floor $\sigma_{\min}=10^{-2}$.
We choose $N_{\mathrm{pop}}=10$ because the per-step action is low-dimensional ($\dim(u)\in\{2,3\}$ depending on the task), so a modest population is typically sufficient for exploration.

*SPSA.*
We use learning rate $10^{-2}$, perturbation magnitude $c=5\times 10^{-3}$, and average $m=2$ independent SPSA perturbation pairs per update.
We choose a relatively large $c$ to obtain a stable finite-difference directional signal under strongly nonlinear deformation; with unit-normalized strip length, $c=5\times 10^{-3}$ corresponds to a small ($\sim 0.5\%$) geometric perturbation that empirically yields more reliable search directions than smaller perturbations that can be dominated by solver tolerance and numerical noise.

## Appendix E System identification for the elastic strip in real robotic manipulation

This part describes the lightweight system-identification procedure used to match the elastic-strip model to the real robot experiments.
The key identified quantity is the gravito-bending length $L_{gb}$, which captures the characteristic deformatino scale of a slender strip under gravity and is robust to many nuisance factors (camera noise, slight boundary mismatch, etc.).

#### Gravito-bending length.

For an inextensible/slender strip under its own weight, the dominant competition is between bending stiffness and distributed gravity load. We parameterize this balance by

$$ $L_{gb}\;:=\;\left(\frac{k_{b}}{\rho whg}\right)^{\tfrac{1}{3}},$ (52) $$

where $k_{b}$ is the bending stiffness (units N$\cdot$m^2), $\rho$ is the density, $w$ is the strip’s width, $h$ is the thickness, and $g$ is gravitational acceleration.

We collect $M=5$ static snapshots of the strip under gravity with diverse boundary conditions (multiple clamp-clamp configurations).
Each snapshot provides an observed equilibrium shape $\hat{x}^{(m)}$ together with the corresponding boundary constraints.
Using multiple configurations reduces degeneracy and discourages overfitting to a single pose.

For a candidate $L_{gb}$, we run the simulator under each experimental boundary condition and obtain the converged equilibrium shape
$x^{(m)}(L_{gb})$.
We measure mismatch on *free nodes only* (excluding constrained/clamped DoFs) and use a mean-squared shape discrepancy:

$$ $J(L_{gb})\;=\;\frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M}\frac{1}{|\mathcal{I}_{f}^{(m)}|}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{I}_{f}^{(m)}}\left\|x^{(m)}_{i}(L_{gb})-\hat{x}^{(m)}_{i}\right\|_{2}^{2},$ (53) $$

where $\mathcal{I}_{f}^{(m)}$ denotes the free-node index set for snapshot $m$.
We identify

$$ $L_{gb}^{\star}\in\arg\min_{L_{gb}\in[L_{\min},L_{\max}]}J(L_{gb}).$ (54) $$

We solve ([54](#A5.E54)) with a bounded 1D scalar minimization (Brent-type method).
Unless otherwise stated, we use bounds $[L_{\min},L_{\max}]=[0.05,0.10]$ and tolerance xatol$=10^{-4}$.
Each objective evaluation runs the forward simulator with gravity enabled and otherwise matches the real-robot settings. The computed $L_{gb}=0.0514\mathrm{m^{-1}}$.
