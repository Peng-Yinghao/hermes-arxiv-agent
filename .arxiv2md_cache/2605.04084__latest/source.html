FASQ: Flexible Accelerated Subspace Quantization for Calibration-Free LLM Compression

Ye Qiao yeq6@uci.edu University of California, IrvineIrvineCaliforniaUSA , Yian Wang yianw11@uci.edu University of California, IrvineIrvineCaliforniaUSA , Zhiheng Chen zhihenc5@uci.edu University of California, IrvineIrvineCaliforniaUSA , Hyoukjun Kwon hyoukjun.kwon.p@gmail.com University of California, IrvineIrvineCaliforniaUSA and Sitao Huang sitaoh@uci.edu University of California, IrvineIrvineCaliforniaUSA
Abstract.

Compressing large language models (LLMs) for deployment on commodity GPUs remains challenging: conventional scalar quantization is limited to fixed bit-widths (e.g., 8/4/3-bit), offers only a few discrete compression points, and typically requires calibration data. We present FASQ (Flexible Accelerated Subspace Quantization), a calibration-free framework that applies product quantization to LLM weight matrices. By tuning two parameters, sub-vector size and codebook cardinality, FASQ exposes a continuous design space spanning 27–49% of the original FP16 model size, filling compression gaps that fixed-bit schemes cannot reach. On Meta-Llama-3-8B, FASQ surpasses 4-bit GPTQ and AWQ in accuracy (67.1–67.7 avg.) at 37–42% model size, with consistent results on Qwen3-8B and Qwen3.5-9B-Base. To make product quantization practical at inference time, we design custom CUDA kernels: a LUT-free direct-compute GEMV for decode and an output-stationary double-buffered LUT GEMM for prefill, both with split-K parallelism. On an RTX 3090, FASQ achieves 45.2 tok/s decode at effective 4-bit (2.56×2.56\times memory reduction) and 51.8 tok/s at effective 3-bit (2.80×2.80\times), both surpassing FP16 tensor-core performance (43.9 tok/s) and delivering 1.61.6–1.8×1.8\times the throughput of AWQ, 2.22.2–2.5×2.5\times of GPTQ, and 4.34.3–5.0×5.0\times of RTN. FASQ is the only compressed method that accelerates decode beyond FP16, offering calibration-free compression, continuous size–quality trade-offs, and real-time inference on a single consumer GPU.

1. Introduction

Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable performance across a wide range of tasks, yet their deployment is constrained by massive memory footprints and high inference costs. A single 7–9 B parameter model requires 14–18 GB of GPU memory in FP16, often exceeding the capacity of commodity GPUs and limiting real-time serving scenarios. Post-training quantization (PTQ) has become the predominant solution: compared to other compression methods that require heavy fine-tuning or retraining, PTQ compresses pretrained weights to lower-precision representations without retraining (gptq, ; awq, ; smoothquant, ; apexq, ).

However, existing PTQ methods share three fundamental limitations. First, scalar quantization is inherently restricted to discrete bit-widths (typically 8, 4, or 3 bits), each yielding a single fixed compression point. Practitioners seeking intermediate trade-offs (e.g., between the 57% model size of INT8 and the 36% of INT4) have no available operating point. Second, state-of-the-art methods such as GPTQ (gptq, ), AWQ (awq, ), and SmoothQuant (smoothquant, ) require representative calibration data to compute Hessian-based corrections, activation-aware scaling factors, or channel migration statistics, respectively. Obtaining such data may be impractical for proprietary or domain-specific models, and calibration procedures introduce a non-trivial step in the deployment pipeline. Third, scalar quantization requires explicit dequantization at inference time, reconstructing FP16 weights before each matrix multiplication. At standard 4-bit precision, custom kernels (e.g., AWQ-W4, GPTQ-W4) amortize this cost effectively, but at non-standard bit-widths where no optimized kernel exists, dequantization overhead can degrade latency by an order of magnitude (gptq, ; awq, ).

Product quantization (PQ), a classical technique from information retrieval that partitions vectors into sub-vectors and encodes each with a shared codebook, addresses all three limitations. By varying the sub-vector size and codebook cardinality, PQ exposes a continuous design space of compression rates. Codebook construction requires only k-means clustering on the weight distribution, eliminating external calibration data. Most importantly, PQ inference operates directly on compressed representations via table lookup, avoiding dequantization entirely and making latency independent of the compression level.

In this paper, we present FASQ (Flexible Accelerated Subspace Quantization), a post-training compression framework that applies multi-codebook product quantization to the linear layers of LLMs. FASQ replaces each FP16 weight matrix with a compact codebook of cluster centroids and a table of uint8 indices, then performs inference directly on these compressed representations: decode uses direct centroid-dot-product accumulation in registers, while prefill uses table-lookup-and-accumulate with shared-memory codebook caching. Through a systematic design space exploration, we show that FASQ covers compression rates from 27% to 49% of FP16 model size with fine granularity, filling the gap between fixed-bit scalar methods.

On Meta-Llama-3-8B, FASQ matches 8-bit quality (68.2 average accuracy across five zero-shot tasks) at 49% model size and exceeds 4-bit AWQ/GPTQ (67.1–68.2 avg.) at 37–42% model size, all without calibration data. These results generalize consistently to Qwen3-8B and Qwen3.5-9B-Base. To make PQ inference practical, we develop custom CUDA kernels: a direct-compute GEMV kernel for decode that eliminates shared-memory lookup tables entirely, and a split-K GEMM kernel for prefill that maximizes GPU occupancy. The resulting engine achieves 45.2 tok/s decode throughput on an RTX 3090, surpassing FP16 (43.9 tok/s) at 2.56×2.56\times less GPU memory. Because FASQ operates directly on compressed representations rather than dequantizing to FP16, its decode latency actually improves at higher compression, unlike scalar methods where non-standard bit-widths require entirely different kernel implementations.

2. Background and Motivation

2.1. Scalar Quantization for LLMs

Scalar quantization reduces the precision of individual weights from floating-point to a lower bit-width integer representation. Given a weight tensor ww, the uniform affine mapping to bb-bit integers is:

(1) Q(w)=clamp(⌊ws⌉+z, 0, 2b−1)Q(w)=\text{clamp}\!\left(\left\lfloor\frac{w}{s}\right\rceil+z,\;0,\;2^{b}{-}1\right)

where s=(max⁡(w)−min⁡(w))/(2b−1)s=(\max(w)-\min(w))\,/\,(2^{b}{-}1) is the scale factor and z=⌊−min(w)/s⌉z=\lfloor-\min(w)/s\rceil is the zero-point. While simple and hardware-friendly, this mapping suffers large quantization errors when outlier values dominate the dynamic range, which is common in LLM attention and feed-forward layers.

To mitigate outlier-induced error, state-of-the-art post-training quantization (PTQ) methods introduce calibration-based preprocessing. GPTQ (gptq, ) performs layer-wise quantization guided by a Hessian approximation computed from a calibration set, greedily rounding weights to minimize reconstruction error. AWQ (awq, ) identifies salient weight columns via activation statistics and rescales them to fit the INT4 range more tightly. SmoothQuant (smoothquant, ) migrates quantization difficulty from activations to weights through per-channel scaling, enabling joint W8A8 quantization. These methods achieve strong quality at INT4 and INT8 but share three structural limitations. First, each bit-width yields a single fixed compression point; no intermediate operating points exist between, e.g., the 57% model size of INT8 and the 36% of INT4. Second, all three require representative calibration data (Hessian statistics, activation distributions, or channel scaling factors) which may be unavailable for proprietary or domain-specific models. Third, scalar quantization requires explicit dequantization at inference time: integer weights must be reconstructed to FP16 before each matrix multiplication, and this cost is amortized only when a dedicated kernel exists for that bit-width. Group quantization partially addresses the first limitation by applying per-group scale factors within a fixed bit-width, but the compression range remains narrow and calibration is still required.

2.2. Product Quantization

Vector quantization (VQ) (vq, ) quantizes entire vectors rather than individual scalars. A codebook of KK centroids is learned via k-means, and each input vector is replaced by the index of its nearest centroid. By capturing correlations within vectors that element-wise methods ignore, VQ yields lower reconstruction error at equivalent storage.

Product quantization (PQ) (pq, ) scales VQ to high-dimensional spaces by partitioning each vector into Ns​sN_{ss} sub-vectors and maintaining an independent codebook per partition. The composite codebook has KNs​sK^{N_{ss}} implicit entries while storing only K×Ns​sK\times N_{ss} centroids, making PQ exponentially more expressive than a single-codebook VQ of the same size. Codebook learning requires only k-means on the data itself, with no external labels or calibration inputs, and the resulting representation is a compact pair of tables: centroids (FP16) and indices (uint8).

Several works have applied PQ or lookup-based computation to neural network inference. Maddness (maddness, ) replaces multiply-accumulate operations with learned hash lookups, and PECAN (pecan, ) uses content-addressable memory for mapping, but both target activations rather than weight compression. DPQ (dpq, ), LUT-NN (lut-nn, ), and PQA (pqa, ) apply PQ to network weights or LLM inference but rely on naïve dequantization without custom GPU kernels, incurring substantial inference overhead. LUT-DLA (lut-dla, ) adds a design-space search for hardware co-optimization but targets FPGA rather than commodity GPUs.

Orthogonally, table-lookup engines on edge FPGAs (tellme, ; pdswap, ; cobra, ) and efficient NAS for constrained platforms (micronas, ; monas, ; tgnas, ; bnn_rimc, ; spatial_arch, ) target custom hardware or extreme quantization, while quantization-induced positional encoding degradation (qroar, ; rope_rescaling, ) further motivates calibration-free approaches. None of these works provide flexible, GPU-efficient compression with a continuous quality and size tradeoff.

3. FASQ

This section describes the FASQ quantization procedure (Section 3.1.1), reconstruction-free inference (Section 3.1.2), and the resulting design space (Section 3.2).

3.1. FASQ Methodology

3.1.1. Quantization

FASQ partitions each weight matrix W∈ℝd×d′W\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d^{\prime}} into Ns​sN_{ss} subspaces along a chosen subspace dimension d​i​ms​sdim_{ss}. The partitioning can be applied along either the row (dd) or column (d′d^{\prime}) axis; we denote this choice by a binary variable d​i​mdim (0 for rows, 11 for columns). The complementary axis is the datapoint dimension d​i​md​pdim_{dp}. Each subspace slice has shape:

with subspace size S​Zs​s=d​i​ms​s/Ns​sSZ_{ss}=dim_{ss}/N_{ss}.

3.1.2. Inference

A naïve approach reconstructs the full weight matrix via index lookups and then calls a standard matrix multiplication. However, reconstruction temporarily materializes the entire FP16 weight matrix, negating the memory savings of quantization.

We instead perform reconstruction-free inference that operates directly on the codebook and index tables without ever materializing the weight matrix. The core observation is that the output of a PQ-compressed linear layer can be decomposed as:

(3) oj=∑s​s=1Ns​sdot​(xs​s,Tcluster​[s​s,Tindex​[s​s,j]])o_{j}=\sum_{ss=1}^{N_{ss}}\text{dot}\!\bigl(\,x_{ss},\;T_{\text{cluster}}[ss,\,T_{\text{index}}[ss,j]]\,\bigr)

where xs​sx_{ss} is the input slice corresponding to subspace s​sss. Each term requires only a single centroid fetch (indexed by TindexT_{\text{index}}) and a small dot product of dimension S​Zs​sSZ_{ss}; no full row of the weight matrix is ever formed.

We specialize the implementation into two kernels tailored to the two LLM inference phases:

  • •

    Decode (GEMV): During autoregressive token generation, the input is a single vector (B=1B{=}1). Each thread owns one output element and directly computes Equation 3 by iterating over subspaces, fetching the relevant centroid on the fly, and accumulating the dot product in a register. No shared-memory lookup table is needed, because each thread uses only one of the KsK_{s} centroids per subspace.

  • •

    Prefill (GEMM): During prompt processing, many input tokens (L≫1L\gg 1) share the same weight. Here, precomputing a shared-memory lookup table LUTs​s​[k]=xl⋅Tcluster​[s​s,k]⊤\text{LUT}_{ss}[k]=x_{l}\cdot T_{\text{cluster}}[ss,k]^{\top} for all KsK_{s} centroids amortizes the cost across the LL output rows, and each thread accumulates results for multiple output features via indexed gathers from the LUT.

Both approaches are inherently dequantization-free: the compressed codebook and index tables are consumed directly, with no intermediate FP16 weight materialization. The detailed kernel designs and CUDA-level optimisations are presented in section 4.

3.2. FASQ Design Space

FASQ targets the linear layers of transformer models, which account for the vast majority of parameters. We assume the baseline model stores weights in FP16 (16 bits per element).

FASQ compresses each layer by replacing the full d​i​ms​s×d​i​md​pdim_{ss}\times dim_{dp} weight matrix with two compact structures: (1) a codebook of Ns​s×KsN_{ss}\times K_{s} centroids, each of dimension S​Zs​sSZ_{ss}, shared across all d​i​md​pdim_{dp} datapoints; and (2) an index table that stores one ⌈log2⁡Ks⌉\lceil\log_{2}K_{s}\rceil-bit integer per datapoint per subspace instead of S​Zs​sSZ_{ss} FP16 values. The per-layer footprint is therefore:

(4) Sfasqlayer\displaystyle S_{\text{fasq}}^{\text{layer}} =16×Ks×d​i​ms​s⏟codebook (shared)+⌈log2⁡Ks⌉×Ns​s×d​i​md​p⏟index table\displaystyle=\underbrace{16\times K_{s}\times dim_{ss}}_{\text{codebook (shared)}}+\underbrace{\lceil\log_{2}K_{s}\rceil\times N_{ss}\times dim_{dp}}_{\text{index table}}

The codebook cost is independent of d​i​md​pdim_{dp} and therefore negligible at typical model dimensions (e.g. <<1% of layer size at Ks=256K_{s}{=}256, d=4096d{=}4096). Compression is dominated by the index table, which replaces each FP16 weight group with a single ⌈log2⁡Ks⌉\lceil\log_{2}K_{s}\rceil-bit index. For a given model and choice of d​i​mdim, the layer size is determined entirely by KsK_{s} and Ns​sN_{ss}.

The model-level size ratio aggregates all FASQ-quantized linear layers together with uncompressed auxiliary components (embeddings, LayerNorm):

(5) Size%=SfasqSbase×100%\text{Size\%}=\frac{S_{\text{fasq}}}{S_{\text{base}}}\times 100\%

We additionally apply deduplication by removing repeated centroids within each subspace; hence Equation 4 is an upper bound. In our tables, the effective bitwidth #W reports only the index rate ⌈log2⁡Ks⌉/S​Zs​s\lceil\log_{2}K_{s}\rceil/SZ_{ss}, excluding codebook overhead, analogous to how integer quantization methods report precision without counting group-wise scaling factors.

We define the FASQ design space as:

(6) Design Space={Ks,Ns​s,d​i​m}\text{Design Space}=\{K_{s},\;N_{ss},\;dim\}

operating on FP16 weights, the standard deployment format for pretrained LLMs. By sweeping S​Zs​sSZ_{ss} from 1 (element-wise k-means) to d​i​ms​sdim_{ss} (full vector quantization) and KsK_{s} from 1 to d​i​md​pdim_{dp}, FASQ traces a continuous Pareto front of compression rate versus model quality (Section 5.2).

4. FASQ Acceleration

LLM inference consists of two phases: prefill, which processes the prompt via a batched GEMM, and decode, which generates tokens one at a time via GEMV. The two phases impose different parallelism requirements, so we design a dedicated CUDA kernel for each. We present the designs of both kernels, an ablation study of the key optimisations, and a sensitivity analysis of FASQ parameters. All microbenchmark measurements are on a single 4096×40964096{\times}4096 linear layer, RTX 3090.

A natural approach to PQ inference is to precompute a lookup table (LUT) of all KsK_{s} dot products per subspace in shared memory, then gather results by index (Equation 3). Whether this pays off depends on the batch size: at B=1B{=}1 each thread uses only one of the KsK_{s} entries, wasting the table; at large LL, many tokens amortize the build cost. This tradeoff drives our two-kernel design.

4.1. GEMV Kernel for Decode

During autoregressive decode, the input is a single token. A naïve LUT approach builds KsK_{s} partial dot products per subspace in shared memory, but each thread consumes only 1 of the KsK_{s} entries; at Ks=256K_{s}{=}256, 99.6% of the LUT is wasted.

Our GEMV kernel (Algorithm 2) eliminates the LUT entirely, as illustrated in Figure 2. Each thread owns a single output element ojo_{j} and iterates over its assigned subspaces. For each subspace s​sss, the thread reads the index k=Tindex​[s​s,j]k=T_{\text{index}}[ss,j] (1 byte), fetches the corresponding centroid Tcluster​[s​s,k]T_{\text{cluster}}[ss,k] (S​Zs​s×2SZ_{ss}\times 2 bytes), computes the dot product with the input slice using S​Zs​sSZ_{ss} fused multiply-add (FMA) operations, and accumulates the result in a register (Figure 2, right). After all subspaces are processed, a single atomicAdd writes the partial sum to global memory.

Split-K parallelism

To maximise GPU occupancy, we partition the Ns​sN_{ss} subspaces across the grid’s z-dimension (Figure 2, left). The auto-tuner targets ∼8{\sim}8 blocks per SM, yielding Ksplit=⌈(NS​M×8)/(Bs×⌈Fout/128⌉)⌉K_{\text{split}}=\lceil(N_{SM}\times 8)/(B_{s}\times\lceil F_{\text{out}}/128\rceil)\rceil splits (∼21{\sim}21 on RTX 3090). Each thread performs a single atomicAdd at the end of its subspace chunk, compared with Ns​sN_{ss} atomics per element in a subspace-stationary design.

Key properties

This design uses zero shared memory and zero synchronisation barriers, enabling up to 12 resident blocks per SM (versus 1 for the 32 KB LUT design). At S​Zs​s=2SZ_{ss}{=}2, the centroid is a single half2 load; the entire subspace codebook (Ks×S​Zs​s×2=1K_{s}{\times}SZ_{ss}{\times}2=1 KB) fits in L1 and is reused across the warp. The dominant cost is reading the index table: Ns​s×Fout×1N_{ss}{\times}F_{\text{out}}{\times}1 B =8=8 MB vs. cuBLAS FP16’s 3232 MB weight read, yielding a 4×4\times memory traffic reduction that explains why our kernel (32​μ32\,\mus) outperforms cuBLAS (45​μ45\,\mus).

4.2. GEMM Kernel for Prefill

During prefill, LL input tokens share the same weight. Building a KsK_{s}-entry LUT per subspace now pays off: the LL tokens each reuse the same table, amortizing the build cost by a factor of LL. Our GEMM kernel assigns one sequence position to each block row (grid-xx) and iterates serially over subspaces. For each subspace, a KsK_{s}-entry LUT is built cooperatively by all threads (each thread computes ⌈Ks/256⌉\lceil K_{s}/256\rceil entries), then each thread looks up 16 output features from the LUT and accumulates results in registers.

Split-K along Ns​sN_{ss}

At short sequences (L=32L{=}32–128), we split the Ns​sN_{ss}-loop across the grid z-dimension to increase block count. At L=128L{=}128 the auto-tuner selects ∼4{\sim}4 splits, boosting blocks from 128 to 512 (6.26.2/SM) and reducing latency by 1.35×1.35\times; at large LL, splits are disabled with zero overhead.

Double-buffered LUT

Two LUT slots reside in shared memory: while accumulators read from lut_cur, lut_nxt is built for the next subspace, requiring only one __syncthreads() per iteration. Each slot is padded to Ks+1=257K_{s}{+}1{=}257 floats (2 KB for the pair) for bank-conflict avoidance.

4.3. Kernel Design Ablation

Table 1 ablates each optimisation cumulatively on a single 4096×40964096{\times}4096 layer.

The baseline PQ kernel (b) uses a shared-memory LUT with subspace-stationary parallelism, where each block owns one subspace and iterates over all output elements. Switching GEMM to an output-stationary design (each thread owns output features, iterates over subspaces) and adding half2 vectorised loads, fused float-to-half conversion, and PyTorch’s caching allocator (c) yields a 1.5×1.5\times GEMV and 1.9×1.9\times GEMM speedup. Eliminating the LUT entirely and adding split-K parallelism along Ns​sN_{ss} (d) achieves a further 2.9×2.9\times GEMV speedup, bringing decode latency below cuBLAS FP16; the GEMM kernel retains its LUT since the cost is amortized across LL tokens. Finally, GEMM split-K (also along Ns​sN_{ss}, but retaining the LUT) and double-buffered LUT (e) provide a 1.35×1.35\times prefill speedup at short sequence lengths.

4.4. Sensitivity Analysis

Codebook size (KsK_{s})

As KsK_{s} varies from 16 to 256 (3(a)), GEMV latency rises only from 25.3 to 37.4 μ\mus (1.48×1.48\times) because the dominant cost, reading the index table (Ns​s×FoutN_{ss}{\times}F_{\text{out}} bytes), is independent of KsK_{s}. All KsK_{s} values remain faster than cuBLAS FP16 (45.5 μ\mus), so compression quality can be tuned freely.

Sub-vector size (S​Zs​sSZ_{ss})

S​Zs​s=1SZ_{ss}{=}1 doubles the index table to 16 MB and disables half2 vectorisation (134.7​μ134.7\,\mus, 3.0×3.0\times cuBLAS). S​Zs​s=2SZ_{ss}{=}2 halves index traffic and enables half2, reaching 36.7 μ\mus (0.81×0.81\times cuBLAS); larger values give diminishing returns. We use S​Zs​s=2SZ_{ss}{=}2 throughout.

Sequence length scaling

Split-K provides the largest GEMM benefit at short sequences (4.5×4.5\times at L=32L{=}32), tapering to 1.01×1.01\times at L≥512L\geq 512 where blocks already saturate the GPU. The FASQ GEMM is ∼26{\sim}26–28×28\times slower than cuBLAS FP16 at large LL (tensor-core gap); however, prefill runs once per request while decode dominates interactive serving (3(c)).

Batch size

At B=1B{=}1, our GEMV kernel is 1.2×1.2\times faster than cuBLAS FP16, as the 4×4\times memory traffic reduction outweighs the lack of tensor cores. As BB grows, cuBLAS scales more favourably via tensor-core GEMM; an adaptive dispatch switching kernels at larger BB is left for future work.

5. Experiments

We evaluate FASQ on an NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU, measuring model quality, compressed model size, and end-to-end inference latency with our custom CUDA kernels.

5.1. Experimental Settings

5.1.1. Baselines

We compare against five post-training quantization methods: GPTQ (gptq, ) (Hessian-guided INT4/INT3), AWQ (awq, ) (activation-aware weight quantization), SmoothQuant (smoothquant, ) (joint weight-activation quantization at W8A8, W6A6, and W4A4), QuIP (quip, ) (incoherence processing before quantization), and RTN (round-to-nearest, no calibration). All baselines except RTN require calibration data; FASQ does not.

5.1.2. LLM Workloads

We evaluate on three modern model families: Meta-Llama-3-8B (llama3, ), Qwen3-8B (qwen3, ), and Qwen3.5-9B-Base (qwen3, ). For scalability analysis, we additionally evaluate on LLaMA-2 (llama2, ) 7B and 13B. We measure perplexity on the WikiText-2 test set (wikitext, ) and zero-shot accuracy on five commonsense reasoning tasks: ARC-easy and ARC-challenge (arc, ), HellaSwag (hellaswag, ), PIQA (piqa, ), and WinoGrande (winogrande, ).

5.1.3. Configurations

FASQ is parameterized by sub-vector size S​Zs​sSZ_{ss} and codebook size KsK_{s}, written as S​Zs​sSZ_{ss}-KsK_{s} (e.g., 2-256 denotes S​Zs​s=2SZ_{ss}{=}2, Ks=256K_{s}{=}256).

5.1.4. Methodology

We reproduced baselines using official implementations of GPTQ111https://github.com/ist-daslab/gptq, AWQ222https://github.com/mit-han-lab/llm-awq, and SmoothQuant333https://github.com/mit-han-lab/smoothquant. All evaluations use lm-evaluation-harness (lm-eval, ). Compression rates are measured from stored model weight size only, excluding activations and KV caches. End-to-end latency is measured on Meta-Llama-3-8B with prompt length 128 and generation length 128. All latency measurements use CUDA event timers with warmup.

Configurations with S​Zs​s≤2SZ_{ss}{\leq}2 form a smooth Pareto front spanning 27–49% of FP16 size, demonstrating the continuous compression–quality tradeoff that FASQ uniquely offers, in contrast to scalar methods that are locked to discrete bit-widths. 5.3. Model Quality Results

5.3.1. Cross-Model Generalization

Table 3 shows that FASQ generalizes across three diverse model families. Across Llama-3-8B, Qwen3-8B, and Qwen3.5-9B-Base, the same configurations produce consistently low perplexity degradation. For example, the 2-1024 configuration adds only 0.2–0.3 PPL above the FP16 baseline on all three models while compressing weights to 49–55% of the original size. Notably, Qwen3.5-9B-Base is especially resilient: even the aggressive 2-128 configuration maintains 7.65 PPL (vs. 6.98 FP16).

5.3.2. Comparison with Baselines

Table 2 presents a comprehensive comparison on Meta-Llama-3-8B, organized by model size. Unlike baselines that are locked to discrete bit-widths (3/4-bit), FASQ covers a continuous range from 27% to 49% of FP16 size.

In the near-lossless region, SmoothQuant W8A8 (56.6% size, Avg 68.2) matches FASQ 2-1024 in quality, but FASQ achieves the same accuracy at 49% size, 8 percentage points smaller, and without calibration data. SmoothQuant W6A6, despite being stored in INT8 containers (also 56.6% actual storage), drops sharply to Avg 64.6. In the 4-bit region, FASQ 2-512 (41.9% size, Avg 68.0) and FASQ 2-256 (37.0% size, Avg 67.8) match GPTQ-4 g128 (35.7%, Avg 67.3) and QuIP-4 (34.8%, Avg 67.1), while SmoothQuant W4A4 collapses entirely (34.8%, Avg 35.5, PPL 4.3E3), demonstrating that joint weight-activation quantization does not scale below 6-bit. This contrast highlights a fundamental advantage of FASQ’s product-quantization design: quality degrades gracefully across the entire compression range (68.2→\to68.0→\to67.8→\to66.0 from 49% to 33% size), whereas SmoothQuant has a single viable operating point (W8A8) and collapses below it (68.2→\to64.6→\to35.5). At 3-bit compression, FASQ 2-128 (33.2%, Avg 66.0) surpasses all 3-bit baselines including AWQ-3 g128 (30.2%, Avg 64.4) and QuIP-3 (29.4%, Avg 63.7). FASQ also uniquely extends below 3-bit: configuration 4-1024 reaches 31.1% size with Avg 66.4, a region no scalar baseline can access.

Critically, FASQ achieves all of these results without any calibration data, relying solely on k-means clustering of weight distributions, while every other baseline (GPTQ, AWQ, SmoothQuant, QuIP) requires a separate calibration pass on representative data. The entire FASQ quantization process (k-means over all layers) completes in approximately 10 minutes on a single GPU.

5.3.3. Scalability

Table 4 demonstrates FASQ’s scalability from LLaMA-2 7B to 13B. Size% shrinks with model scale: the 4-1024 configuration reaches 24.1% at 7B and 21.9% at 13B, while PPL degradation remains modest (6.04 vs. 5.47 at 7B; 5.36 vs. 4.88 at 13B). This trend suggests that FASQ will be especially effective for larger models where the codebook overhead is further amortized.

5.4. End-to-End Inference Performance

Table 5 reports end-to-end latency on Meta-Llama-3-8B using real model inference on an RTX 3090. We compare FASQ against FP16 tensor-core inference and all baselines with their respective official inference kernels.

5.4.1. The Dequantization Overhead Problem

A key finding is that weight-only quantization alone does not improve inference latency compared to FP16 tensor-core execution. Naïve RTN quantization at 4-bit achieves only 10.4 tok/s decode, which is 4.2×4.2\times slower than FP16 (43.9 tok/s), because the runtime must dequantize INT8 containers back to FP16 before calling cuBLAS, doubling the memory traffic and adding a full intermediate-materialization step. SmoothQuant W8A8 faces a similar bottleneck: despite using torch._int_mm for real INT8 GEMM on tensor cores, the per-token activation quantization and dequantization overhead limits decode to 16.4 tok/s (W6A6 is identical since it uses INT8 containers). Note that SmoothQuant targets a different tradeoff by quantizing both weights and activations; however, both W8A8 and W6A6 occupy 56.6% of FP16 in actual storage. At this cost, FASQ 2-1024 (49%, Avg 68.2) matches W8A8 quality in a smaller footprint, and far exceeds W6A6 (Avg 64.6). These results confirm that without specialized GPU kernel support, quantization can actually hurt latency.

5.4.2. Previous Work’s Fused Kernels

To overcome dequantization overhead, GPTQ and AWQ implement custom CUDA kernels that fuse weight unpacking with the matrix multiplication. AWQ’s W4A16 kernel (awq, ) packs two 4-bit weights into one byte and fuses the unpack, dequantization, and FP16 GEMM into a single kernel launch, eliminating the intermediate FP16 weight materialization. GPTQ employs a similar fused-kernel strategy through optimized backends (gptq, ). These fused kernels substantially improve over naïve dequantization: AWQ 4-bit achieves 28.1 tok/s decode (vs. RTN’s 10.4 tok/s) with 2.80×\times memory savings. However, even with kernel fusion, no scalar quantization method matches FP16 decode speed (43.9 tok/s), because the dequantize-then-multiply paradigm inherently requires additional ALU operations per weight element.

5.4.3. FASQ Kernel Performance

In contrast, FASQ’s CUDA kernels (section 4) achieve FP16-parity decode speed while delivering substantial memory savings. At effective 4-bit precision (S​Zs​s=2SZ_{ss}{=}2, Ks=256K_{s}{=}256), FASQ achieves 45.2 tok/s decode, surpassing FP16 (43.9 tok/s) while using 2.56×\times less memory (5,975 MB vs. 15,317 MB). At effective 3-bit (S​Zs​s=2SZ_{ss}{=}2, Ks=128K_{s}{=}128), decode speed actually exceeds FP16 at 51.8 tok/s with 2.80×\times memory savings. This is possible because the GEMV kernel reads only the compact index table (Ns​s×Fout=8N_{ss}\times F_{\text{out}}=8 MB per layer at S​Zs​s=2SZ_{ss}{=}2) rather than the full FP16 weight matrix (32 MB), achieving a 4×4\times reduction in memory traffic that directly translates to proportional speedup in the memory-bandwidth-bound decode phase.

Compared to the best-performing baseline kernel, FASQ’s decode throughput is 1.6×\times faster than AWQ 4-bit (45.2 vs. 28.1 tok/s), 2.2×\times faster than GPTQ 4-bit (vs. 20.5 tok/s), 2.8×\times faster than SmoothQuant W8A8 (vs. 16.4 tok/s), and 4.3×\times faster than naïve RTN (vs. 10.4 tok/s). FASQ is the only compressed method that does not sacrifice decode speed for memory savings.

The prefill phase is slower than FP16 (935 ms vs. 41 ms at Ks=256K_{s}{=}256) because the LUT-based GEMM cannot leverage tensor cores. However, in interactive serving the total latency is dominated by decode: at generation length 128, decode accounts for over 75% of end-to-end time, and this share grows with longer outputs. For deployments that prioritize time-to-first-token (TTFT), a hybrid dispatch that reconstructs FP16 weights for prefill via cuBLAS and switches to FASQ’s GEMV kernel for decode achieves both fast TTFT and compressed-model decode speed, at the cost of temporarily materializing the full weight matrix during prefill.

5.4.4. Structural Advantages

Beyond raw performance, FASQ offers two advantages that no scalar baseline provides. First, FASQ covers a continuous compression range from 27% to 49% of FP16 size by adjusting S​Zs​sSZ_{ss} and KsK_{s}, all served by the same CUDA kernel, whereas scalar methods require a different kernel for each bit-width. Second, FASQ requires zero calibration data, eliminating distribution-mismatch risk and enabling instant quantization of any model, including proprietary ones where representative data is unavailable.

6. Conclusion

We presented FASQ, a calibration-free LLM compression framework based on product quantization. By tuning sub-vector size and codebook cardinality, FASQ spans 27–49% of FP16 model size with accuracy matching or exceeding GPTQ, AWQ, and SmoothQuant across Llama-3-8B, Qwen3-8B, and Qwen3.5-9B, all without calibration data. Our LUT-free GEMV kernel reduces memory traffic by 4×4\times versus FP16, achieving 45.2 tok/s decode on an RTX 3090, surpassing FP16 (43.9 tok/s) at 2.56×2.56\times less memory and outperforming every baseline kernel (1.61.6–4.3×4.3\times faster). FASQ is the only compressed method that does not sacrifice decode speed for memory savings.

Limitations and future work.

The prefill kernel remains slower than cuBLAS because LUT-based accumulation cannot leverage tensor cores. Two directions may close this gap: (1) a pipelined reconstruction kernel that rebuilds FP16 weight tiles in parallel with tensor-core GEMM, avoiding full materialization while exploiting hardware matrix units; and (2) Triton-based code generation with tiling strategies tailored to the PQ access pattern. On the compression side, per-layer parameter assignment and post-training codebook fine-tuning could further improve accuracy at aggressive compression rates.
